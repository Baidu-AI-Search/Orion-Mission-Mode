## Geographic Pattern Analysis

### 1. The Rust Belt / Industrial Midwest dominates

Ohio, Pennsylvania, Michigan, Indiana, and Illinois together take **5 of the top 7 spots** by dollar volume, and **5 of the top 6 by payee count**. This reflects several converging historical factors:

- **Legacy of federal manufacturing & defense installations.** The Midwest was a major hub for WWII and Cold War defense production — military arsenals, tank/auto plants (e.g., the Detroit Tank Arsenal), Air Force logistics centers (Wright-Patterson AFB in Ohio), and Army depots (Tobyhanna in PA, Letterkenny, Ravenna). These installations employed massive blue-collar federal/civil-service workforces who are now fully retired.
- **CSRS-era workforce.** Many of these retirees entered federal service under the older **Civil Service Retirement System (CSRS)**, which provides more generous defined-benefit pensions than the modern FERS system. That drives up average pension dollars per recipient.
- **Aging-in-place populations.** Unlike Sun Belt states to which retirees migrate, many Midwestern federal retirees stayed local after careers at nearby federal facilities, concentrating pension outflows in those states.

### 2. Large-population states appear, but not strictly by population

California (#5), New York (#6), and Florida (#3) all rank high, consistent with their large populations — however, **Texas (#11, $168M / 26,159 payees)** and **Virginia (#17, $110M / 22,524 payees)** rank well below what their populations would predict. This is counterintuitive at first glance (Virginia and Maryland are adjacent to D.C. and heavy with current federal employees), but makes sense because:

- The data counts **retirees receiving benefits**, not current employees. Many career federal workers in the D.C. metro retire to lower-cost or warmer states (Florida, the Carolinas, Pennsylvania, Ohio) rather than staying in Northern Virginia or Maryland.
- Texas's federal workforce footprint is comparatively younger and tied to more modern installations with fewer long-tenured CSRS retirees.

### 3. Florida and the Sun Belt — migration magnet effect

Florida ranks **#3** on both lists despite not hosting a proportional share of *current* federal employment — this is the classic **retirement migration effect**: federal retirees from across the Northeast and Midwest relocate to Florida for climate, tax advantages (no state income tax), and cost of living. North Carolina (#9) and Georgia (#10) show the same dynamic, capturing outflow from the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic.

### 4. The smallest states are sparsely populated and/or federal-light

The bottom five — North Dakota, D.C., Wyoming, Alaska, South Dakota — combine **small populations** with **limited federal installation footprints** (Alaska has military bases but a small overall retiree base; the Dakotas and Wyoming are rural with few large federal civilian employers). D.C. is a special case: home to current workers but not to many retirees, who tend to leave the District upon retirement.

### 5. Territories and overseas are negligible

Puerto Rico is the largest territorial recipient (~$10.4M, 3,427 payees) — modest but visible. Guam, the U.S. Virgin Islands, American Samoa, Northern Mariana Islands, and overseas military designations (AA, AE, AP) collectively total under $1M, confirming that federal pension dollars are overwhelmingly paid to recipients residing in the 50 states.

## Summary Takeaways

- **$5.71 billion** in annual federal pension payments flow to **877,305 current retirees**, with another **483,720 deferred** future payees in the pipeline.
- The **Midwest/Rust Belt (OH, PA, MI, IN, IL)** is the epicenter of federal pension outflows, driven by legacy defense and civil-service employment and retirees who stayed local.
- **Pennsylvania** has the most federal pensioners; **Ohio** pays out the most dollars, signaling slightly richer average benefits.
- **Florida** and the Southeast benefit from retiree in-migration; **Texas and Virginia** underperform their population due to out-migration of retirees and younger federal workforces.
- The smallest, most rural states (Dakotas, Wyoming, Alaska) and D.C. itself anchor the bottom.
