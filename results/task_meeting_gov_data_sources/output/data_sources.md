# Data Sources & Measurement Systems Referenced at NASA's First Public UAP Meeting

This document catalogs the data sources, sensors, databases, and measurement systems that were explicitly referenced during NASA's May 31, 2023, public meeting of the Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena (UAP) Independent Study Team. Each entry captures how the source was discussed in the context of UAP research, its owner/operator, and any stated limitations.

Source transcript: `transcript.md` (Rev.com transcript of the NASA public meeting).

## Category Details

### 1. Government/Military Sensors

#### 1. AARO UAP Case Database / Holdings
- **Owner/Operator:** All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO), U.S. Department of Defense (Director: Sean Kirkpatrick).
- **Description:** Centralized repository of UAP reports and associated sensor data drawn from across the U.S. government; contains both classified and unclassified material with emphasis on national security areas.
- **Relevance to UAP:** The core whole-of-government UAP catalog. At the time of the meeting it held more than 800 cases (up from ~650 reported to Congress the prior month), growing by roughly 50–100 new reports per month; the recent jump was attributed to integration of FAA data. AARO uses it to drive analytic trends (morphology, altitude, location heat maps, characteristic "target packages").
- **Limitations:** Only a very small percentage (~2–5%) of cases display signatures that could reasonably be described as anomalous; many remain unresolved due to insufficient data. The heat map is heavily collection-biased (reflects where U.S. military/IC/FAA sensors are located, not necessarily where UAP occur). No space-domain reports and no maritime reports had been collected at that time.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (primary); David Spergel.

#### 2. DOD Operational Military Sensors (airborne, ground, sea)
- **Owner/Operator:** U.S. Department of Defense and the Military Services.
- **Description:** Operational sensors across airborne, ground-based, and sea-based military platforms used for detecting, tracking, and targeting objects.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Supply the vast majority of UAP reports currently in AARO's holdings. AARO is surveying whether these sensors can detect and characterize the "typical UAP target" (≈1–4 m, 0–Mach 2, orbs/spheres predominant).
- **Limitations:** These are **not** scientific instruments. They are purpose-built to identify known objects and put weapons on them; they are not calibrated for scientific measurement, lack consistent metadata, and can produce sensor artifacts (defocusing, apparent motion due to platform movement, range misperception). DOD cannot lawfully point the full collection apparatus at domestic backyards.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick; David Spergel; Nicola Fox.

#### 3. Intelligence Community (IC) Sensors
- **Owner/Operator:** U.S. Intelligence Community.
- **Description:** High-precision, calibrated intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) sensors.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Kirkpatrick noted that IC sensors, unlike operational DOD sensors, are "very close to scientific sensors — calibrated, high precision, everything you'd ever want to know about a thing," and thus provide the highest-quality classified UAP observations.
- **Limitations:** Data is classified primarily because of collection method, not content (e.g., a spy-satellite image of a balloon is classified). Cannot be routinely released to the scientific community.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick; David Spergel.

#### 4. MQ-9 Reaper EO/IR Sensor Pod
- **Owner/Operator:** U.S. Air Force (operated via DoD).
- **Description:** Electro-optical/infrared (EO/IR) full-motion video sensor mounted on the MQ-9 remotely piloted aircraft.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Footage shown from a 2022 MQ-9 mission in the Middle East captured the archetypal "spherical metallic orb" that Kirkpatrick described as AARO's most commonly reported morphology — illustrating the "typical UAP" case.
- **Limitations:** In the example shown, the object demonstrated no enigmatic technical capabilities and posed no flight-safety threat; limited resolution and contextual data prevented definitive identification without additional observations.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

#### 5. P-3 Maritime Patrol Aircraft Sensors
- **Owner/Operator:** U.S. Navy (operated via DoD).
- **Description:** Surveillance EO/IR and other sensors on the P-3 Orion maritime patrol aircraft.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Newly released P-3 training-mission video from the western U.S. showed three apparent "dots" the aircrew could not intercept. Cross-referencing with air traffic control data revealed they were ordinary commercial aircraft on a flight corridor — a concrete example of how range misperception and platform motion can spoof highly trained aircrews and sensors.
- **Limitations:** Sensor defocusing, platform-induced apparent motion, and difficulty judging range create false-anomaly impressions.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick; Nicola Fox.

#### 6. F-35 Electro-Optical Targeting System (EOTS) / Cameras
- **Owner/Operator:** U.S. Military (Joint Strike Fighter program).
- **Description:** Aircraft-mounted EO/IR cameras used for targeting.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Cited by Spergel as an example of why military-camera data is often classified: even a picture of a bird taken by an F-35 camera is classified because of the sensitivity of the sensor. Used to illustrate the broader classification problem that impedes open scientific study.
- **Limitations:** Daytime-optimized, not designed for scientific measurement; subject to ghosting from off-axis sun reflections; classified by default.
- **Who referenced it:** David Spergel; Sean Kirkpatrick (in calibration-testing context).

#### 7. AARO Purpose-Built Dedicated UAP Sensors
- **Owner/Operator:** AARO (U.S. DoD).
- **Description:** New sensors AARO has "purpose built" and is deploying in select surveillance locations, designed specifically to detect, track, and characterize objects matching the typical UAP "target package."
- **Relevance to UAP:** Part of AARO's scientific plan to augment existing DOD/IC/commercial/civil/NASA/NOAA sensors. Initial deployment is described as a "bootstrap method" — broad-spectrum search across a small number of indicators to gather data, then refine.
- **Limitations:** Early-stage capability; must be calibrated against known objects; constrained by U.S. law regarding domestic surveillance.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

### 2. Civilian Aviation Systems

#### 8. FAA Air Route Surveillance Radar Model 4 (ARSR-4)
- **Owner/Operator:** Federal Aviation Administration with joint DoD homeland-defense use.
- **Description:** Long-range (~200–250 nautical mile) en-route radar; altitude coverage up to ~100,000 ft. Sited roughly equally around the perimeter of the continental U.S.
- **Relevance to UAP:** One of the non-cooperative surveillance systems contributing to AARO's civilian-airspace data feed and the FAA hot-spot coverage picture.
- **Limitations:** Line-of-sight geometry; detection-range models assume a ~1 m² radar-cross-section target (comparable to an F-15/F-18); coverage is geometrically screened by terrain (notably in the mountainous West); clutter and biological-filtering remove low/slow/non-aircraft returns.
- **Who referenced it:** Mike Freie (FAA).

#### 9. FAA Common/Air Route Surveillance Radar (CRSR / CARSR)
- **Owner/Operator:** Federal Aviation Administration.
- **Description:** Long-range en-route radar system located in the interior of the U.S. (alongside ARSR-4s on the coasts). Joint use for air-traffic control and homeland security.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Part of the non-cooperative long-range continental radar coverage that contributes to FAA/AARO air situational awareness.
- **Limitations:** Same as other non-cooperative radars (line-of-sight, filtering, 1 m² target assumption).
- **Who referenced it:** Mike Freie (FAA).

#### 10. FAA Airport Surveillance Radar (ASR) – Short-Range Radars
- **Owner/Operator:** Federal Aviation Administration.
- **Description:** Short-range (~40–60 nautical mile) radars with altitude detection up to ~24,000 ft, sited at high- and medium-density airports.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Provide terminal-area non-cooperative surveillance; contribute to the domestic UAP-reporting baseline.
- **Limitations:** Concentrated where airports/population exist (denser east of the Mississippi, sparser in the West); detectability drops sharply for small targets (drones ~0.01 m²); rotation rates of 12 RPM (terminal) or 5 RPM (en route) — no tasked/steered mode, constant search.
- **Who referenced it:** Mike Freie (FAA); Federico Bianco.

#### 11. FAA Automatic Dependent Surveillance–Broadcast (ADS-B)
- **Owner/Operator:** Federal Aviation Administration.
- **Description:** Cooperative surveillance technology in which equipped aircraft broadcast their GPS position; FAA's preferred primary data source for airspace management.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Provides a cooperative-track "ground truth" reference layer that can resolve apparent UAP tracks by matching them to known aircraft. Used by Kirkpatrick's analysts to de-conflict the P-3 "three dots" case. Strong nationwide coverage down to ~1,500 ft AGL.
- **Limitations:** Only detects aircraft *equipped with ADS-B out*; cooperative-only by definition — i.e., it cannot detect unknown/non-cooperative objects, which is precisely the UAP set. General aviation VFR flights without ADS-B are invisible to it.
- **Who referenced it:** Mike Freie (FAA); Sean Kirkpatrick.

#### 12. FAA Surface Movement / Airport Surface Surveillance Systems
- **Owner/Operator:** Federal Aviation Administration.
- **Description:** Radar and multilateration systems for tracking aircraft and vehicles on airport surfaces.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Listed by Freie for completeness; not a primary UAP-detection source but part of the FAA's overall surveillance enterprise.
- **Limitations:** Restricted to airport surfaces; not relevant to airborne UAP.
- **Who referenced it:** Mike Freie (FAA).

#### 13. FAA Domestic Events Network (DEN) Reporting System
- **Owner/Operator:** Federal Aviation Administration.
- **Description:** The internal FAA function/organization to which air traffic controllers report unusual events (including UAP sightings).
- **Relevance to UAP:** Receives and archives controller UAP reports; feeds into AARO's database. Historically ~3–5 reports per month nationwide from 14,000 controllers; rose to 8–10/month in Aug 2022 (possibly Starlink-related) and to 68+ reports in Feb 2023 after the Chinese balloon incident.
- **Limitations:** Report volume is very low relative to daily operations (~45,000 operations/day); relies on voluntary controller reporting; pilot-side reporting process is less clear.
- **Who referenced it:** Mike Freie (FAA); Mike Gold.

#### 14. FAA En Route Automation Modernization (ERAM) / STARS Systems
- **Owner/Operator:** Federal Aviation Administration.
- **Description:** The processing/display systems (ERAM at en-route centers, STARS at terminal facilities) that fuse cooperative and non-cooperative radar inputs into the controller's traffic picture.
- **Relevance to UAP:** These are the systems where retained processed FAA radar data lives; they are the practical source for any retrospective FAA UAP analysis.
- **Limitations:** Data retained is the *processed/filtered* display product, not raw I&Q radar returns; raw I&Q data volumes are described as prohibitively large and costly to retain. Retention period measured in months (for legal/accident-investigation purposes), not years.
- **Who referenced it:** Mike Freie (FAA); Walter Scott; Reggie Brothers; Warren Randolph.

#### 15. National Weather Service (NWS) Weather Balloon Network
- **Owner/Operator:** NOAA / National Weather Service (referenced in FAA context).
- **Description:** 92 NWS weather stations release radiosonde balloons twice daily (00Z and 12Z), each flight roughly 2 hours, ascending to ~100,000 ft before the envelope bursts; at least ~184 NWS balloon flights per day CONUS, plus university/hobbyist balloons.
- **Relevance to UAP:** A known mundane source of UAP reports; launches are pre-coordinated with local ATC and tracked via GPS. Cited as an example of background clutter that must be filtered out.
- **Limitations:** University and hobbyist balloons are less consistently tracked; all balloons can appear as slow-moving radar targets at high altitude.
- **Who referenced it:** Mike Freie (FAA); Reggie Brothers.

### 3. Space-Based Assets

#### 16. NASA Earth-Sensing Satellites (Earth Science Division)
- **Owner/Operator:** NASA Earth Science Division.
- **Description:** Constellation of NASA satellites used for Earth system science (atmosphere, oceans, land, weather).
- **Relevance to UAP:** Kirkpatrick explicitly asked NASA to evaluate whether Earth-sensing satellites can detect objects matching the typical UAP target package; Paula Bontempi described the Earth Science Division as a major existing asset base for studying atmospheric phenomena relevant to UAP characterization. Shelley Wright noted that a formal analysis of which NASA assets could resolve a ~1–4 m, Mach 0–2 target had **not yet been done**.
- **Limitations:** Current NASA assets are tuned to large, slow geophysical phenomena (Walter Scott: "really big haystacks that are moving very slowly"); frame rates and spatial/spectral resolution are not optimized for small fast-moving objects; low frame rate is a known issue for fast targets.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick; Paula Bontempi; Shelley Wright; Walter Scott; Nicola Fox.

#### 17. NASA Radiological Sensors
- **Owner/Operator:** NASA.
- **Description:** Sensors capable of measuring ionizing radiation / radiological signatures.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Kirkpatrick listed radiological sensors among NASA's unique capabilities that AARO would like to leverage; potentially relevant if UAP produce anomalous radiation signatures.
- **Limitations:** Not yet applied systematically to UAP; unknown phenomenology (Mike Gold noted we do not yet know what signature — radiation, EM, etc. — to look for).
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

#### 18. NASA Gravitational-Wave Detection Tools
- **Owner/Operator:** NASA (in partnership with NSF / LIGO and the broader gravitational-wave community).
- **Description:** Instruments and data-analysis pipelines for detecting gravitational waves.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Cited by Kirkpatrick as an example of a sensing modality NASA has access to that could potentially be brought to bear on the UAP problem, without specifying a concrete use case.
- **Limitations:** Not designed for atmospheric/aerospace object detection; no established UAP signature model.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

#### 19. NASA Geomagnetic Sensors / Tools
- **Owner/Operator:** NASA.
- **Description:** Instruments for measuring geomagnetic/electromagnetic-wave phenomena.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Listed alongside gravitational-wave and radiological capabilities as a NASA asset that may help detect or characterize unusual signatures associated with UAP.
- **Limitations:** Phenomenology undefined; not yet configured for UAP searches.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

#### 20. Hubble Space Telescope
- **Owner/Operator:** NASA / ESA (operated by NASA).
- **Description:** Visible/UV/near-IR space telescope in low Earth orbit.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Used by Spergel as an illustrative example of a well-calibrated scientific instrument that nonetheless produces well-known optical artifacts (saturation bleed, off-axis "ghost" reflections, dust-scatter diffuse halos) — making the point that even high-quality sensors produce "funny phenomena" that must be understood in context before being treated as anomalies. Must **never** be pointed at the Sun; analogous care is needed when interpreting any sensor data.
- **Limitations:** Artifacts from bright sources, internal reflections, dust scattering; not a UAP sensor; used to illustrate general calibration principles.
- **Who referenced it:** David Spergel.

#### 21. James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)
- **Owner/Operator:** NASA / ESA / CSA.
- **Description:** Infrared space observatory at Lagrange Point L2.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Cited alongside Hubble as an example of a calibrated scientific telescope whose data still contains artifacts, reinforcing the need for well-characterized instruments for any serious UAP work.
- **Limitations:** Same general artifact/calibration caveats; designed for deep-space astronomy, not atmospheric targets.
- **Who referenced it:** David Spergel.

#### 22. International Space Station (ISS) – High-Speed Imaging Platforms
- **Owner/Operator:** NASA / international partners.
- **Description:** Low-Earth-orbit platform with high-speed cameras used for Earth and atmospheric observation.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Spergel cited high-speed imaging, "often from places like the Space Station," as key to confirming sprites (upward lightning) — a historical case of pilot reports initially being dismissed until high-frame-rate data validated the phenomenon — held up as a model for how UAP reports might be resolved.
- **Limitations:** Existing cameras are not purpose-configured for UAP patrols.
- **Who referenced it:** David Spergel.

#### 23. NOAA Earth-Observing Satellites / Sensors
- **Owner/Operator:** National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA).
- **Description:** Operational and research weather/environmental satellites.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Explicitly named by Kirkpatrick alongside NASA sensors as existing civil space-based assets whose utility for the "typical UAP target" should be evaluated.
- **Limitations:** Designed for meteorological/oceanographic observations, not small fast-moving objects; formal sensitivity/capability analysis not yet performed.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

#### 24. Commercial Satellite & Airborne Sensor Data
- **Owner/Operator:** Commercial remote-sensing / aerospace industry (multiple providers).
- **Description:** Commercial Earth-observation satellites and airborne platforms.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Kirkpatrick noted AARO's survey extends beyond DOD/IC sensors to commercial and civil sensors; the panel discussed commercial data as a future augmentation.
- **Limitations:** Variable calibration and metadata; tasking costs; coverage patterns not optimized for UAP.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

### 4. Ground-Based Scientific Instruments

#### 24 (general class). Large-Scale Ground-Based Scientific Instrumentation
- **Owner/Operator:** Worldwide scientific / academic community (multiple observatories and labs).
- **Description:** The global collection of calibrated large-scale ground-based scientific instruments across multiple phenomenologies (radio, optical, IR, radar, etc.).
- **Relevance to UAP:** Kirkpatrick recommended NASA lead an evaluation of whether these instruments — though not designed for UAP work — could serendipitously detect the typical UAP target package and provide tip-and-cue triggers. Archived calibrated data from these instruments is proposed as a target for AI/ML anomaly searches.
- **Limitations:** None were designed for UAP detection; sensitivity to a 1–4 m fast-moving object is unquantified; point detectors will be needed to "point up, point out" for active search.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

#### 25. Australian Radio Observatory (FRB-detection radio telescope)
- **Owner/Operator:** Australian academic research observatory (name not specified in transcript).
- **Description:** A sensitive radio telescope in Australia used for fast radio burst (FRB) research.
- **Relevance to UAP:** An anecdote from Spergel: the observatory detected bursts with "really strange structure" that bunched around lunchtime — ultimately traced to staff opening microwave-oven doors early, which leaked radio pulses. Used as a cautionary lesson that sensitive instruments can detect mundane terrestrial RFI, and that unexpected signals are often conventional, though sometimes (like genuine FRBs) they are major discoveries.
- **Limitations:** Susceptible to radio-frequency interference from everyday human activity; illustrates the need for rigorous RFI characterization.
- **Who referenced it:** David Spergel.

#### 35. DOE/DOD National Laboratories (modeling & simulation)
- **Owner/Operator:** U.S. Department of Energy and Department of Defense National Laboratories.
- **Description:** National-lab capabilities for modeling & simulation, fundamental-physics analysis, and sensor-signature modeling.
- **Relevance to UAP:** AARO is partnering with DOE/DOD labs to explore the state of the art in fundamental physics relevant to the small subset of cases with anomalous motion/propulsion signatures — modeling what signatures (maneuverability, speed, propulsion, signature management) should be expected.
- **Limitations:** Applied to a small percentage of cases; findings are not yet public.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

### 5. Crowdsource / Public Data

#### 30. NASA UAP Public Reporting Website (science.nasa.gov/uap)
- **Owner/Operator:** NASA (Office of Communications / UAP Independent Study Team).
- **Description:** NASA's public-facing website for UAP information; portal through which public questions were solicited for the meeting and through which NASA is releasing answers over time.
- **Relevance to UAP:** The public channel for questions about NASA's UAP study; the panel acknowledged receiving "hundreds and hundreds" of public questions through the site. Not itself a sensor, but the central public interface around NASA's UAP data effort.
- **Limitations:** At the time of the meeting it was a Q&A/informational site rather than a structured crowdsourced reporting platform.
- **Who referenced it:** Karen Fox.

#### 31. Consumer Smartphones (iPhone and other mobile devices)
- **Owner/Operator:** General public / citizen scientists.
- **Description:** Consumer smartphones with cameras, GPS, accelerometers, and other onboard sensors.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Kirkpatrick stated that imagery from an iPhone is "generally not helpful unless you are right up on whatever it is you're looking at," but that ancillary metadata (location, speed, other sensor readings) from multiple devices could be very helpful. Walter Scott asked whether NASA should open-source cell-phone apps with proper metadata capture.
- **Limitations:** Uncalibrated sensors; poor ranging; standalone imagery is of limited scientific value; requires coordinated multi-observer capture.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick; Walter Scott; David Spergel.

#### 32. Proposed Crowdsourcing Citizen-Science Platform
- **Owner/Operator:** NASA (proposed; not yet built at time of meeting).
- **Description:** A recommended centralized, coordinated platform (potentially leveraging smartphone apps and other sensors) with curated data ingestion, best-practice guidance, metadata standards, and a network to "follow up" sightings by tipping-and-cueing other citizen sensors.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Federica Bianco argued that simple guidance documents are insufficient — a platform is needed to reach a large community, centralize/curate data, and allow follow-up coordination when a sighting occurs. Kirkpatrick also identified crowdsourcing/unclassified open-source data as a key area where the public can help.
- **Limitations:** Not yet implemented; requires broad community uptake and coordination; needs rigorous metadata and curation standards to be useful.
- **Who referenced it:** Federica Bianco; Walter Scott; Sean Kirkpatrick.

#### 33. Open-Source / Public Unclassified Data
- **Owner/Operator:** Public domain / multiple open sources.
- **Description:** Publicly available unclassified data of diverse origins.
- **Relevance to UAP:** NASA's independent study is specifically evaluating unclassified, public-facing data sources (in contrast to AARO's classified holdings). Distributions of civilian sightings outside national-security areas could be compared to AARO's classified distribution map to build a holistic picture (Kirkpatrick).
- **Limitations:** Fragmented, unsystematic, often from uncalibrated instruments; subject to high background (aircraft, drones, balloons, ionospheric phenomena) and to reporting stigma (e.g., commercial pilots reluctant to report).
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick; David Spergel.

### 6. Databases / Archives

#### 26. NASA Historical Records / Archives
- **Owner/Operator:** NASA (including NASA historian's office).
- **Description:** NASA's institutional records, mission data, and astronaut reports held across NASA centers.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Public questions asked whether the NASA historian had been tasked to review NASA's historical records for UAP evidence. David Spergel responded that the panel's charge was forward-looking (a roadmap), and the team had "not done a complete historical study in an archive," but noted the value of learning from reported events (resolved and unresolved) to design future data collection.
- **Limitations:** Systematic historical archival review had not been performed for this study; astronaut Scott Kelly testified that, in his 20 years in the astronaut corps, there were no formal or informal briefings or NDAs regarding UAP.
- **Who referenced it:** Karen Fox; David Spergel; Scott Kelly; public questions.

#### 27. U.S. Government UAP Historical Records
- **Owner/Operator:** DoD / IC / AARO (across agencies).
- **Description:** Historical U.S. government records related to UAP across defense and intelligence agencies.
- **Relevance to UAP:** AARO is "meticulously reviewing" U.S. government historical UAP records as part of its mission; this was part of Kirkpatrick's congressional testimony.
- **Limitations:** Some remain classified; quality and curation of historical records vary.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

#### 28. Archived Calibrated Scientific Data (worldwide)
- **Owner/Operator:** Global scientific agencies and institutions.
- **Description:** The world's archive of calibrated, large-scale scientific data from instruments of multiple phenomenologies.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Kirkpatrick recommended applying AI/ML tools to mine these archives for anomalous signatures that correlate with reported UAP events — described as an "interesting study" to leverage past data.
- **Limitations:** Data were collected for other purposes; searches depend on having a defined anomaly signature; computational and access challenges.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

#### 29. Five Eyes Allied Data / Foreign Partner Data
- **Owner/Operator:** Five Eyes intelligence alliance (U.S., UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand) and other foreign partners.
- **Description:** Data-sharing arrangements under discussion/development following AARO's first Five Eyes UAP forum.
- **Relevance to UAP:** Kirkpatrick reported that the first Five Eyes forum on UAP had just been held; partners were discussing data sharing, reporting procedures, shared analysis, and mutual calibration of capabilities. Foreign partners are expected to feed data into AARO's process; Kirkpatrick also called for expanding military/intelligence collaborations into scientific/academic communities worldwide.
- **Limitations:** Early stage; data-sharing agreements still being established; scope of scientific community inclusion remains to be built out.
- **Who referenced it:** Sean Kirkpatrick.

## Cross-Cutting Themes from the Meeting

- **Calibration is everything.** Across virtually all sources — DOD weapons sensors, FAA radars, commercial cameras, consumer phones, even flagship space telescopes — the panel emphasized that sensors must be well characterized and calibrated against known objects before anomalous signals can be trusted. AARO is building a large calibration test matrix flying known objects (e.g., weather balloons) past F-35s and other sensors under varied conditions.
- **Stigma and under-reporting.** David Spergel and Mike Freie both noted commercial pilots and controllers may under-report; stigma degrades the civilian data stream.
- **Current data is fragmented and unsystematic.** Across agencies, existing UAP data come from instruments not designed for UAP, lack curation, and face a large "background" of mundane explanations (aircraft, drones, balloons, sprites/ionospheric events, balloons, airborne clutter, sensor artifacts, even microwave ovens).
- **The dominant recurring UAP "target package"** (per Kirkpatrick) is roughly 1–4 m in size, at aircraft altitudes, at speeds 0–Mach 2, with ~half of reported morphologies being round/spherical "orbs." This target profile frames which sensors — military, civil, space, ground — might plausibly detect it.
- **Multi-modal, coordinated, tip-and-cue collection** (using one sensor detection to rapidly task many others — including academic/citizen sensors) was repeatedly identified as the desired future architecture, supported by AI/ML for anomaly mining and for modeling the "normal" pattern of life.
- **NASA's unique civilian, open-data role.** Because DOD/IC data is largely classified due to collection method, NASA's open, well-characterized data streams and its linkages to the academic/scientific community were seen as critical complements to AARO's classified work.
