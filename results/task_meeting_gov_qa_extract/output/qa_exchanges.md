## Part 1 — Panel-to-Presenter Q&A

### 1. Expanding sensor coverage beyond national-security assets
- **Questioner:** Reggie Brothers (panel member; introduced as "Reggie")
- **Respondent:** Sean Kirkpatrick (Director, All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office, AARO)
- **Topic:** Sensor expansion & legal/operational constraints
- **Question:** AARO's existing sensors are built for national-security threats and particular phenomenologies/frequency ranges. Is there a need to go beyond that, and what challenges (materials, etc.) do you see?
- **Answer:** Yes — AARO is already purpose-building new sensors that search across a broader set of characteristics tied to pilot-reported signatures, using a "bootstrap" approach: begin with a broad-spectrum search on a few indicators, get more data, then narrow. AARO cannot simply point the full U.S. collection apparatus at random backyards (legal/privacy constraints); ground-based "point up" detectors are therefore needed. AARO is also evaluating existing/under-construction sensors in academia and industry that modeling-and-simulation shows could plausibly see the targets, and looking at recalibrating them.

### 2. Size of the AARO database and share of truly anomalous cases
- **Questioner:** Dr. Nadia Drake (panel member, science journalist)
- **Respondent:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
- **Topic:** Case counts and "anomalous" percentage
- **Question:** How large is the database? Over how many years? When the director says only a "very small percentage" or "few objects" show potentially anomalous signatures, what numbers does "few" refer to?
- **Answer:** At the time of his open Senate hearing the database was ~650 cases; it is now over 800. AARO receives roughly 50–100 new reports per month. A recent jump came from finally integrating FAA data, which added ~100+ cases. The annual report due to Congress on August 1 will have updated unclassified numbers. The share of cases that appear "possibly really anomalous" is in the low single digits — roughly 2–5% of the total database.

### 3. Interpreting the "three white dots" FLIR-style video
- **Questioner:** Nicola Fox (panel member; identified later as NASA Heliophysics Division Director / Associate Administrator)
- **Respondent:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
- **Topic:** Video interpretation / sensor artifacts
- **Question:** Watching the clip with three spots moving while background white spots look stable: the three were identified as airplanes, but what are all the stable white dots in the background? The three spots appear to be moving together — are they flying in formation or is that sensor motion?
- **Answer:** The stable white background is a star field. The three "moving" aircraft were ~30–40 miles away (a smaller plane passing at the bottom was much closer, ~6–10 miles). The apparent motion is sensor jitter; the dots were on an equally-spaced flight line and the sensor was jittering as it tracked, which made them appear to move in unison.

### 4. Single vs. multi-sensor estimation of size/speed and the "trends" slide
- **Questioner:** Walter Scott (panel member; identified as "Walter")
- **Respondent:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
- **Topic:** Sensor ranging & physical characterization
- **Question:** On the "typically reported characteristics" slide (size, altitude, speed) — if an object is seen by only a single sensor and range is unknown, how can size or speed be assessed? Are those reported values actually real?
- **Answer:** Only partially. Not all cases are single-sensor; many are multi-sensor, and the slide parameterizes the full range of values that have been observed across all cases.

### 5. Sensor/data-processing artifacts in the "meatball" MQ-9 clip
- **Questioner:** Walter Scott (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
- **Topic:** Sensor artifact checks / data provenance
- **Question:** For the clip showing the round "meatball" object traversing the frame, was sensor artifact or data-processing artifact ruled out?
- **Answer:** Yes — it is an electro-optical (EO) sensor on an MQ-9, the sensor is very well understood, and the object is real.

### 6. AARO's use of AI / machine learning
- **Questioner:** Anamaria Berea (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
- **Topic:** AI/ML techniques applied to UAP data
- **Question:** You mentioned using AI/ML on existing datasets, including open-source data — what kinds? Anomaly detection? Natural-language processing? Computer vision?
- **Answer:** AARO is researching a span of capabilities but has not yet deployed them operationally. Plans include: (a) NLP on pilot reports; (b) target-recognition / computer-vision models trained on known objects so analysts can retrospectively query the holdings ("give me how many of these you have"); (c) AARO is not yet doing real-time active targeting via ML because the team does not yet have enough confidence in what known classes look like — with the explicit exception of balloons, where filtering is being pushed aggressively.

### 7. Definition of "anomalous" and impact of stigma (incl. NASA's role)
- **Questioner:** Mike Gold (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
- **Topic:** Operational definition of anomalous; stigma and harassment
- **Question:** (a) Among the small subset of cases flagged as truly unexplained, what phenomenology leads you to label them anomalous? (b) How damaging is stigma, and what specifically can NASA do to help?
- **Answer (stigma first):** Stigma has improved since the Navy took up the issue years ago but has not disappeared — including inside government leadership. Kirkpatrick and his team have faced substantial harassment, especially after his prior public hearing, from people demanding immediate answers and refusing to accept the scientific method; this harassment itself feeds the stigma. NASA's biggest contribution is to lead the scientific discourse in an open environment like this public meeting, and to elevate the conversation so researchers can work without harassment.
- **Answer ("anomalous"):** AARO has formal definitions shared with the White House and Congress and partly written into law. In short, "anomalous" means anything not readily understandable by the operator or the sensor — e.g., maneuvering against the wind at Mach 2 with no apparent propulsion, or objects appearing to enter the water (a case that was later traced to a sensor artifact and will be published). It is essentially a signature-management problem: if a thing in the field of view does not reflect light or behave the way it should, it is flagged.

### 8. International / Five-Eyes partnerships
- **Questioner:** Speaker 1 (unidentified panel member)
- **Respondent:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO); David Spergle (panel chair, follow-up clarification)
- **Topic:** International data-sharing partnerships
- **Question:** You recommended foreign partnering with NASA — have you already partnered with international agencies, and are there channels for them to report to AARO?
- **Answer:** AARO just held its first Five-Eyes forum on UAP (with Dan Evans present) and is beginning data-sharing discussions — reporting mechanisms, analysis support, calibration support, mutual aid. Partners will send data into AARO's process. Beyond the Five Eyes, Kirkpatrick has not had bandwidth to expand and would look to NASA to leverage its scientific/academic relationships with allies and partners. Clarifying for the audience: Five Eyes = UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, United States.

### 9. FAA radar data retention and mode
- **Questioner:** Walter Scott (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Mike Freie (FAA)
- **Topic:** Radar data retention, raw vs. processed, scan mode
- **Question:** (a) Does FAA retain radar data or cycle over it? Raw or processed? (b) Are the radars operated in a "tasked" mode or constant search?
- **Answer:** (a) There is long-term retention (driven by legal/accident-investigation requirements), measured in months; the retained data is processed display data from systems such as the en-route ERAM system or the terminal STAR system, not raw INQ returns. (b) Current systems have fixed faces rotating continuously at either 12 RPM (terminal) or 5 RPM (en-route) — constant search, not a taskable mode.

### 10. Baseline rate of FAA controller UAP reports
- **Questioner:** Unidentified panelist (transcript labels the speaker line as "Mike Freie," but context shows a panelist asking)
- **Respondent:** Mike Freie (FAA)
- **Topic:** Baseline UAP reporting rate from controllers
- **Question:** You said three to five reports per month — out of how much activity?
- **Answer:** Three to five per month across ~14,000 controllers nationwide, against ~45,000 operations per day. A very small percentage of total operations. After Starlink launches in August 2022 the rate ticked up to ~8–10/month; the Chinese balloon incident in February triggered a much larger spike (68 reports beginning that month).

### 11. Stigma in pilot/controller reporting
- **Questioner:** Federica Bianco (panel member; transcribed as "Federico Bianco")
- **Respondent:** Mike Freie (FAA)
- **Topic:** Stigma and reporting bias in FAA channels
- **Question:** Do you encourage reporting? Do you feel stigma around UAPs is impairing reporting? Is reporting biased?
- **Answer:** The reporting procedure is written into the air-traffic controller order — controllers who see something they cannot identify have a defined process to report it to the Domestic Events Network (DEN). Freie is not aware of specific stigma or limitations in the controller workforce beyond that process, but acknowledged he is not best positioned to speak to cultural barriers.

### 12. Radar-site placement / geographic coverage
- **Questioner:** Federica Bianco (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Mike Freie (FAA)
- **Topic:** Geographic distribution of radar coverage
- **Question:** The coverage map looked strong on the coasts but had gaps elsewhere — how are radar sites chosen?
- **Answer:** Short-range Airport Surveillance Radars (ASRs) are sited at airports, so coverage is denser in the more-populated eastern half of the U.S. and sparser in the West. Long-range radars are more evenly spaced for homeland defense and high-altitude en-route cruise coverage.

### 13. Radar filtering of ground clutter and biologicals
- **Questioner:** Warren Randolph (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Mike Freie (FAA)
- **Topic:** Radar filtering and what FAA deliberately does not see
- **Question:** FAA isn't actually trying to detect everything — can you speak to filtering techniques?
- **Answer:** Pointing a radar lower covers lower altitudes but picks up trees and other ground clutter. FAA's mission is the safe operation of manned aircraft, so filters are tuned to a "sweet spot." Systems also filter out slow/biological targets (insects, dust, birds, leaves) so controllers see a clean display. Filter settings have been optimized over many years; they can be adjusted but are set to remove non-aircraft returns.

### 14. Capturing raw INQ data for post-hoc calibration
- **Questioner:** Reggie Brothers (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Mike Freie (FAA)
- **Topic:** Feasibility of collecting raw radar data
- **Question:** Following Warren's point, would it be possible to collect the raw radar data so that post-hoc calibration (as Sean described) could recover phenomenology that filtering removes?
- **Answer:** "Raw" INQ (I/Q) data is enormous — very high volume/gigabyte scale — and retaining it would likely be cost-prohibitive; FAA does not do it today. FAA does record data, but it is data that has already been through processing/filtering. With enough time and money raw collection is technically feasible, but would require modifying systems or adding new collection paths — not without significant technical and programmatic challenge.

### 15. Tracking and characterization of daily weather/research balloons
- **Questioner:** Reggie Brothers (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Mike Freie (FAA)
- **Topic:** Characterization of the ~184 daily balloon flights
- **Question:** You cited ~184 balloon flights per day — are those characterized (tracked, cooperative), or does FAA just know they are launched and they drift?
- **Answer:** Under Part 101 regulations, balloons are well tracked. National Weather Service balloons: prior to release, the local ATC facility is notified with launch time; tracking data is provided throughout flight. Most commercial balloons transmit GPS position so controllers know where they are and can separate them from air traffic. University/hobbyist balloons are launched as well, but are typically smaller.

### 16. Non-cooperative-surveillance anomalies and VFR/non-transponding traffic
- **Questioner:** Shelley Wright (panel member), with follow-up
- **Respondent:** Mike Freie (FAA)
- **Topic:** Non-cooperative (primary radar) returns and anomaly handling
- **Question:** (a) Beyond the 3–5/month controller reports, have there been anomalies from non-cooperative surveillance sensors themselves, and what is the process for those? (b) Follow-up: could those returns represent unregistered flights?
- **Answer:** (a) FAA's mission is not anomaly-hunting on non-cooperative sensors. Controllers merge non-cooperative (primary radar) and cooperative (transponder/ADS-B) targets and display them with a confidence level (non-cooperative-only vs. merged) — FAA does not separately flag "anomalies" from primary radar. (b) General-aviation VFR flights without cooperative avionics are indeed picked up by non-cooperative radar and shown to controllers; a "flight following" procedure lets pilots request controller awareness via radio even without a transponder.

### 17. Pilot UAP reporting process and archiving
- **Questioner:** Mike Gold (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Mike Freie (FAA)
- **Topic:** Pilot reporting channels & archiving
- **Question:** (a) If I am a pilot and see a UAP, where do I find the reporting process? (b) When FAA reports these incidents, to whom are they reported, and are they archived (into Sean's database, etc.)?
- **Answer:** (b) Reports are sent to the Domestic Events Network (DEN), an FAA function; Freie believes they are archived and presumes they flow into AARO's database. (a) Freie is familiar with the controller-side process (written into the controller order) but did not know the pilot-facing reporting path offhand.

### 18. UAP as an opportunity to teach the scientific method
- **Questioner:** Federica Bianco (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Dr. Paula Bontempi (panel member / oceanographer, NASA Earth Science background)
- **Topic:** Public engagement & scientific literacy
- **Question:** NASA has great visibility and UAPs draw a very diverse public; can you speak to the opportunity for NASA to expand understanding of the scientific method through this study?
- **Answer:** UAPs create multiple opportunities: convening interdisciplinary teams of scientists and citizen scientists to look objectively at NASA's data archives; renewing federal, international, and private-sector partnerships; and using high public interest as a vehicle to demonstrate how data, archives, and evidence-based reasoning actually work.

### 19. Definition of "anomalous" for citizen-science purposes
- **Questioner:** Shelley Wright (panel member)
- **Respondents:** Dr. Paula Bontempi (lead); Dr. Nadia Drake (follow-up)
- **Topic:** Defining "anomalous" beyond operator/sensor framing
- **Question:** Kirkpatrick defined "anomalous" as "not readily understandable to the operator or the sensor." For a NASA panel, especially when thinking about citizen science, do we need a broader definition and how do we communicate that?
- **Answer (Bontempi):** Not all archive data is user-friendly out of the gate, but NASA provides browse/quick-look products, and there is room to help the public engage with environmental/satellite data around sightings even without overnight expertise; communication and engagement are central to de-stigmatization.
- **Answer (Drake):** The phrase "not readily understandable" is doing a lot of work; for the panel's purposes NASA will want a slightly more specific definition of what "anomalous" actually means.

### 20. Crowdsourcing / citizen-science platforms
- **Questioner:** Walter Scott (panel member)
- **Respondent:** Federica Bianco (panel member; astrophysicist/data scientist)
- **Topic:** Crowdsourcing design (apps, metadata, follow-up networks)
- **Question:** On crowdsourcing and citizen-scientist reports — are you thinking of guidelines only, or open-sourcing cell-phone apps that capture the right metadata? What does that look like?
- **Answer:** Guidelines alone are insufficient; what is needed is a platform. Cell phones have been used for crowdsourcing in astrophysics/space science and could work here, but the platform must reach a large enough community, connect observers so a sighting can trigger multi-observer follow-up (pointing more sensors at the event), and centralize/curate the incoming data and metadata.

### 21. Synthetic data for AI/ML training
- **Questioner:** Speaker 4 (unidentified panel member)
- **Respondent:** Federica Bianco (panel member)
- **Topic:** Risks of synthetic training data
- **Question:** Given limited quality data, could synthetic data be generated (filling in characteristics based on what we know) to train neural networks/AI to iterate on correlations as real data arrives?
- **Answer:** Data scientists generate synthetic data when real data are sparse, but it is risky for anomaly detection — synthetic data embed the creators' own biases about what data and anomalies "should" look like, which can bias models toward what we already expect and cause genuinely novel signals to be missed.

### 22. Cross-community collaboration (astrobiology, Earth science, UAP)
- **Questioner:** Dr. Paula Bontempi (panel member)
- **Respondent:** David Grinspoon (panel member; planetary scientist/astrobiologist)
- **Topic:** Bridging astrobiology, Earth-science and UAP communities
- **Question:** Astrobiology does related anomaly/biosignature work on Earth, not just elsewhere — what are your thoughts on bringing together communities that don't normally collaborate, both to establish "normal" baselines and to help explain reported UAP?
- **Answer:** Strongly in favor. Most of what astrobiology studies is on Earth (our only example of an inhabited planet), and insights/collaborations across Earth science, astrobiology, and other communities would be very valuable.

### 23. Lessons from the de-stigmatization of technosignatures/SETI
- **Questioner:** Shelley Wright (panel member)
- **Respondent:** David Grinspoon (panel member)
- **Topic:** Stigma lessons from SETI/technosignatures
- **Question:** NASA has only recently begun supporting technosignature research after decades of stigma. Are there lessons from the technosignature/SETI community that apply to UAP/solar-system studies?
- **Answer:** Yes. Technosignatures were long kept at arm's length by NASA because of stigma, but a curiosity-driven agency trying to understand the whole universe eventually has to move past stigmas and look honestly at evidence; the same lesson applies to UAPs.

### 24. Data-standardization gap between biosignatures/technosignatures and UAP
- **Questioner:** Speaker 5 (unidentified panel member)
- **Respondent:** David Grinspoon (panel member)
- **Topic:** Data standardization and ML readiness
- **Question:** Biosignatures and technosignatures have well-standardized datasets that support ML/AI, while UAP data do not — should the UAP field learn from how those communities handle data?
- **Answer:** Partially push back: classical radio/optical SETI does have large long-standing datasets, but "technosignatures" as a broader/newer category (looking for passive technological signatures rather than deliberate signals) does not yet have deep standardized data. Still, the broader point holds: lessons from astrobiology and classical SETI on standardized, analysis-ready data should inform how UAP data are collected.

### 25. Future sensor desiderata / NASA assets inventory
*(These exchanges occurred in the open panel discussion after the formal presentations, immediately before the public Q&A; they are included here as panel→presenter exchanges because they are substantive Q&A between the chair and panel members.)*
- **Questioner:** David Spergle (panel chair)
- **Respondents:** Dr. Paula Bontempi; Shelley Wright; Walter Scott
- **Topic:** Future data/sensor recommendations and current NASA asset utility
- **Question:** Looking to the future — what data would you want, and how would you want to collect it? What wavelengths, what characterization? (Framing for recommendations.)

### 27. Transparency, NDAs, and "what is NASA hiding?"
- **Questioner:** Public (curated by Karen Fox)
- **Respondents:** David Spergle (chair; one-word "No"); Dan Evans (NASA Designated Federal Official); Scott Kelly (panel member; former NASA astronaut); David Grinspoon (panel member)
- **Topic:** Openness/transparency; astronaut NDAs; alleged cut NASA TV feeds
- **Question (bucket):** What is NASA hiding and where? How much has been shared publicly? Has NASA ever cut live NASA TV to hide UAPs? Has NASA released all UAP evidence it has received? Do astronauts have NDAs/clearances preventing them from talking about UAP sightings?
- **Answer (Evans):** NASA is "cast iron" committed to openness, transparency, and honesty; live NASA TV feeds have never to his knowledge been intentionally cut to hide anything (including UAPs). Interruptions happen because space is complex (natural phenomena, human-made objects, etc.). The very holding of this public televised meeting is evidence of that commitment.
- **Answer (Scott Kelly):** In 20 years in the astronaut office, he never had any formal or informal NDA-style briefing or discussion about UAPs/UFOs, nor any reports of anything suggesting extraterrestrial origin.
- **Answer (Grinspoon):** The culture of science is inherently rebellious against authority; scientists question orthodoxy as part of doing good science. A mass cover-up across the scientific community is inconsistent with that culture — if ordered to hide something, many scientists would be more, not less, motivated to disclose it.

### 29. Reducing stigma around UAP reporting
- **Questioner:** Public (curated by Karen Fox)
- **Respondents:** Karlin Toner (FAA / panel member); Mike Gold (panel member); Dan Evans (NASA)
- **Topic:** De-stigmatization strategy
- **Question (bucket):** What is being done to address the stigmatization surrounding UAP study?
- **Answer (Toner):** The very fact that NASA convened this public panel and committed to transparency is the first step to normalizing UAP study; refining terminology (UAP vs. UFO, "Anomalous" vs. "Aerial") is part of moving beyond a cultural framing that discourages reporting.
- **Answer (Gold):** NASA should participate in symposia/panels and sponsor research; putting the NASA logo on that work normalizes it. Stigma is not just a science/discovery issue but a national-security one — rival nations' balloons have already demonstrated that stigma can be exploited as a vulnerability.
- **Answer (Evans, agency perspective):** Three lines of effort: (1) rigorous, evidence-based, expert-led science (legitimizes the field); (2) public meetings and open dialogue (normalizes discussion); (3) cross-government collaboration — especially with AARO — to demonstrate a serious, whole-of-government approach.

### 33. Extraterrestrial life, astrobiology, and what NASA would do if evidence emerged
- **Questioner:** Public (curated by Karen Fox)
- **Respondents:** David Spergle (panel chair); David Grinspoon (panel member); Dr. Paula Bontempi (panel member); closing comments by David Spergle
- **Topic:** Whether NASA has found ET life; distinction between astrobiology, technosignatures and UAP; reporting channels for the public
- **Question (bucket):** Has NASA encountered aliens/extraterrestrial life? What happens if the public or NASA discovers ET life? What would NASA do?
- **Answer (Spergle):** "Is there life out there?" is one of NASA's core questions, driving solar-system exploration and exoplanet work; planets are common and so are potential habitable environments. But: we have not found life beyond Earth yet. NASA is not hiding information — answering "are we alone?" is precisely the kind of question NASA and its scientists are excited to address. AARO remains the lead federal entity for UAP reporting. Most UAP reports will resolve to known phenomena (balloons, commercial aircraft, drones); some are national-security issues (e.g., the Chinese balloon), which is another reason people should report. NASA's role includes removing stigma, raising data quality (provenance, multi-sensor, high frame rate, multiple perspectives), and potentially enabling citizen science (e.g., a smartphone-based app that centrally and uniformly collects data — billions of phone cameras can help eliminate optical artifacts like ghosting when multiple views are combined).
- **Answer (Grinspoon):** Astrobiology thinks often about "what if we succeed?" Scientists are highly motivated to share real evidence of extraterrestrial life — that is the goal. Historical example: in the 1990s, when researchers thought they saw fossil microbes in the Martian meteorite ALH 84001, they did not announce it immediately; they worked hard to avoid a false alarm, and when they were confident they held a major public event (a Presidential press conference with President Clinton). That illustrates the pattern: be careful, get it right, then announce loudly and publicly.
- **Answer (Bontempi):** Without wading into "aliens," astrobiology/exobiology uses Earth's extreme environments (polar cold, volcanoes, deep-ocean black smokers) as analogs for other worlds, and Earth's oceans still yield new species that look "alien." NASA's former "Oceans Across the Solar System" initiative is an example of the interdisciplinary synergies Earth/astrobiology can bring to future data collection.
