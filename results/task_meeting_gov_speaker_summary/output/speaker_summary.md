# Speaker Summary — NASA's First Public Meeting on UAPs

*Source: `transcript.md` — Deliberative meeting of NASA's UAP Independent Study team held under FACA.*

Speakers are listed in the order their substantive remarks/presentations begin. Minor procedural interjections and unidentified speakers are grouped at the end under **Panel Q&A Contributions**.

---

## 1. Dan Evans — Designated Federal Official, NASA Science Mission Directorate

**Key points:**
- Called the meeting to order, introduced the 16-member UAP Independent Study panel, and paid tribute to retired Air Force MSgt Sam Sato (intelligence collection manager for UAP analysis), who was being laid to rest at Arlington that day.
- Condemned online harassment of panel members; confirmed a NASA security team is addressing the abuse, noting that harassment undermines the scientific process.
- Framed NASA's role as applying rigorous, unclassified, data-driven scientific scrutiny to UAP, complementary to AARO's classified/national-security work.
- Stressed that no UAP program or programmatic budget exists yet; the study team's budget is consistent with other external NASA advisory groups. Funding decisions will follow the final report.
- Reassured the public that NASA is committed to transparency and has never intentionally cut live feeds to hide UAP; feed interruptions reflect the complexity of space operations.
- Described NASA's efforts to destigmatize UAP study: rigorous evidence-based methodology, public meetings/open dialogue, and interagency collaboration (especially with AARO).

**Notable quotes:**
> "It's critical to understand any form of harassment towards our panelists only serves to detract from the scientific process which requires an environment of respect and openness."
> "To my knowledge, NASA has never intentionally cut a live feed to hide anything, and that includes UAPs."

## 2. Nicola Fox — Associate Administrator, NASA Science Mission Directorate

**Key points:**
- Doubled down on Dan Evans's condemnation of panelist harassment; described it as obstructing the public's right to knowledge and discouraging scientific progress.
- Noted that NASA Administrator Bill Nelson asked the agency to examine unclassified civilian data and determine what NASA can contribute to UAP understanding.
- Clarified the role distinction: DOD/Intelligence Community data is often classified *because of collection methods*, not because of what is captured; NASA's strength is open, well-calibrated, publicly available data.
- Framed the study as hypothesis-driven science: even without knowing exactly what we are looking for, known hotspots and trends give a starting point from which to untangle the "chicken-and-egg" problem.
- Walked the panel through eight charge questions as a draft high-level framework for the final report (covering civilian-government data archives, commercial/nonprofit data, future collection, crowdsourcing, AI/ML, public engagement, etc.).
- Emphasized that the search for life beyond Earth is a core NASA theme; UAP study is part of a broader curiosity-driven endeavor and that NASA is "not hiding anything."
- Indicated the final public report was targeted for the end of July.

**Notable quotes:**
> "While we don't know all of the possible outcomes in the entire world, we can ask very specific questions and go about it in a very scientific process to understand."
> "We haven't found life beyond Earth yet. Let's be clear about this, we haven't found it yet. But we're looking and we're looking for it in lots of different ways."

## 3. David Spergel — Chair, NASA UAP Independent Study Team; astrophysicist, Simons Foundation/Princeton

**Key points:**
- Framed the panel's mission as helping NASA identify how it can contribute its unique strengths (open data, calibrated sensors, scientific community relationships) to the UAP question alongside AARO's leadership.
- Stated clearly that existing UAP data is "unsystematic and fragmented" across agencies, often collected with instruments uncalibrated for science, and that eyewitness reports alone are insufficient; high-quality, multi-sensor, well-curated data is essential.
- Noted that background clutter (commercial aircraft, drones, balloons, military equipment, ionospheric effects) is large and must be characterized before anomalies can be identified.
- Emphasized that the change from "Aerial" to "Anomalous" in UAP broadened scope to space, air, and undersea; because NASA's purview extends across the solar system, that broader framing is relevant.
- Used the "needle-in-a-haystack" metaphor: anomaly detection works best when you know "hay" extremely well, not when you search only for a preconceived needle.
- Stated plainly that **no extraordinary evidence of non-human intelligence has been seen**; extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence.
- Encouraged citizens who see something unusual to report to AARO (the lead agency), and described NASA's role as helping set high data-quality standards, reduce stigma, and draw the broader scientific community into the work.

**Notable quotes:**
> "We have not seen the extraordinary evidence. In a sense, to make the claim that we see something that is evidence of non-human intelligence would require extraordinary evidence. And we have not seen that."
> "If you know the properties of hay very well…you don't need to have a match filter looking for a needle in a haystack if you know hay very well."

## 4. Sean Kirkpatrick — Director, All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO)

**Key points:**
- Thanked NASA for convening the study; described NASA and AARO as complementary partners — NASA works with unclassified/civilian data, AARO handles classified/national-security datasets, and both are committed to the scientific method and data-driven analysis.
- Outlined AARO's progress since its July 2022 establishment: standardized reporting, rigorous review of historical UAP records, data-sharing with Five Eyes partners (first Five Eyes forum held that week), and use of scientific/analytic approaches.
- Stressed that resolving UAP cases *cannot* be done by DOD/IC alone; NASA's world-class scientists, earth-sensing satellites, radiological/gravitational/geomagnetic sensors, crowdsourcing tools, and academic linkages are essential.
- Listed specific areas he wanted NASA to help evaluate: commercial satellite/radiological/other sensor data; crowdsourcing via smartphones (including metadata like location/speed); large-scale ground-based scientific instruments (FRB-style serendipitous detection); Earth-science satellites; tip-and-cue sharing with the scientific/academic community; peer review of advanced physics capabilities; AI/ML mining of archived calibrated scientific data; sighting-distribution maps combining unclassified and classified views; and expanding foreign partnerships into academic/scientific communities.
- Clarified that AARO is *researching* AI/ML (NLP for pilot reports, target recognition) but has not deployed real-time AI targeting because they first need confidence in what phenomena to train on.
- Reinforced that sensor/platform effects (gimbal jitter, parallax, motion of the sensing aircraft) frequently create illusions of anomalous motion — citing a newly released three-aircraft video as an example of platform-induced jitter/background-stabilization artifacts.

**Notable quotes:**
> "The resolution of all UAP cases cannot be accomplished by DOD and the intelligence community alone."
> "I should be able to hand that same tip and cue to the scientific and academic community."

## 5. Mike Freie — Federal Aviation Administration (FAA)

**Key points:**
- Presented an overview of FAA's National Airspace System (NAS): more than 14,000 controllers, 520 air-traffic-control towers, 147 TRACONs, 21 en-route centers, over 19,000 airports, ~16 million flights per year (~45,000/day), 25 million general-aviation hours annually.
- Clarified that the FAA's primary mission is safe, efficient control of **manned aircraft**; sensors, filters and displays are designed around that mission — not around detecting UAP.
- Explained surveillance systems: primary surveillance radar (non-cooperative, rotating at 5 RPM en-route / 12 RPM terminal), secondary/ADS-B (cooperative), and surface surveillance.
- Discussed coverage: denser in the eastern U.S. (more population/airports); long-range radars evenly spaced for homeland defense and high-altitude en-route coverage.
- Explained filtering trade-offs: pointing radar lower sees more low-altitude targets but more ground clutter (trees, etc.); filters strip out "biologicals" (insects, dust, leaves, birds) to keep controller displays clean — this optimization intentionally suppresses non-aircraft returns.
- Noted known clutter sources: ~184 weather-balloon flights per day (92 stations × twice daily) plus hobbyist/university balloons; drones; and other non-manned entrants operate on a "not-to-interfere" basis.
- Reported UAP sighting statistics: historically 3–5 controller reports per month; an August 2022 uptick (8–10) possibly linked to Starlink launches; a major spike to 68 reports beginning February 2023 after the Chinese balloon incident.
- On "raw data" collection: true unprocessed (I/Q) radar data is extremely high-volume and cost-prohibitive to store routinely; the FAA currently saves processed/filtered data. Collecting true raw data is technically feasible but would require system modifications and funding.

**Notable quotes:**
> "Our mission is around manned aircraft and safe and efficient operation of those."
> "Historically, those [UAP reports] have been in the range of about three to five reports per month…with the Chinese balloon incident in February, we did see a significant uptick. There's 68 UAP reports that started in February."

## 6. Dr. Nadia Drake — Panel member; science journalist and physicist (afternoon session framing)

**Key points:**
- Framed the afternoon panel discussions and provided a conceptual framework for thinking about UAP.
- Noted the definitional shift mid-study: "A" in UAP changed from "Aerial" (atmosphere only) to "Anomalous" (space, air, and undersea); the panel had largely focused on the aerial domain where most sightings cluster, but the broader definition matters for NASA's scope.
- Emphasized three core points:
  1. Credible witnesses (pilots, military) have reported objects with peculiar behavior, which is why this warrants serious study.
  2. The vast majority of UAP reports ultimately end up with prosaic explanations (balloons, aircraft, drones, atmospheric/optical effects).
  3. For the small remainder that resist identification, we need better, calibrated, multi-sensor data — and NASA is well positioned to help.
- Stressed the language burden carried by the phrase "not readily understandable" and urged the panel to develop a more precise definition of "anomalous."

**Notable quotes:**
> "UAP initially stood for Unidentified Aerial Phenomena…That A is now defined as Anomalous, which includes the space, air, and undersea domains."
> "I was struck by the phrase 'not readily understandable.' I felt like that was actually doing a lot of work."

## 7. Dr. Paula Bontempi — Panel member; oceanographer / Earth scientist

**Key points:**
- Addressed "why NASA": NASA is a science-driven agency with over 60 years of experience measuring phenomena in air and space from the unique vantage point of space, including Earth-system (atmospheric, aquatic) observations.
- Highlighted NASA's vast, freely available archive of satellite, ground-network and other asset data, plus advanced capabilities in modeling, computation, visualization and data analysis — all applicable to UAP investigation.
- Noted that NASA Earth-science assets study the atmosphere and oceans; while Kirkpatrick had indicated *no* reported UAP below the ocean surface to date, NASA's Earth-observation community and data could be brought to bear if reports arise.
- Cautioned that not all archived data is "user-friendly" out of the box, but browse/quick-look products let citizens and researchers contextualize sightings.
- Questioned whether interdisciplinary science efforts have ever been deliberately focused on *anomaly detection* (vs. expected phenomena like hurricanes or algal blooms) — an open research need.
- Noted the value of bridging disjoint research communities (e.g., Earth remote-sensing and astrobiology/biosignature communities) in a way that could benefit UAP analysis.

**Notable quotes:**
> "NASA primarily is a science-driven agency. It's committed to exploring and understanding air and space. And this includes…the unknown, whether that's the farthest reaches of the universe or right here on our home planet."
> "To [Kirkpatrick's] knowledge, and I think to ours, there isn't anything that's been reported below the ocean surface."

## 8. Dr. Federica Bianco — Panel member; astrophysicist and data scientist, University of Delaware

**Key points:**
- Emphasized that scientific discovery requires data meeting the **FAIR** standard — Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable — and that existing UAP data *do not* meet it: collection is inconsistent, inhomogeneous, uncalibrated, poorly documented, incomplete, and not systematically retrievable.
- Argued that poor data quality is a bottleneck for applying modern AI/ML/anomaly-detection methods.
- Called for a strategic, coherent network of calibrated, homogeneous instruments rather than ad hoc sensors; a central curated data repository; and standardized metadata collection.
- Advocated a **citizen-science / crowdsourcing platform** (not just guidelines), noting smartphone sensors are already used in astrophysics and space science. Key requirements:
  - A platform that reaches a large enough community to be useful.
  - Two-way coordination so that if one person sights something, a broader network can be tipped/cued to point sensors.
  - Centralized curation of incoming data.
- Warned on **synthetic data**: used routinely in data science to augment sparse datasets, but in anomaly detection it risks baking in our biases about what anomalies look like, causing ML to miss the truly unknown.
- Clarified that anomaly detection algorithms don't need to know what the anomaly looks like; they work by building a solid model of "normal" and flagging outliers.
- Citizen reports alone are of limited value because the human visual system is easily fooled; systematic calibrated observations are required.

**Notable quotes:**
> "I think just a set of guidance of best practices…would just not be sufficient. I think what needs to be provided is a platform."
> "You can find things that you don't know how they look…a lot of the algorithms in anomaly detection are really based on let's find out how what we know looks like so that anything that doesn't look like that can be identified."

## 9. Dr. David Grinspoon — Panel member; planetary scientist and astrobiologist

**Key points:**
- Connected UAP study to astrobiology and SETI: NASA missions already search for biosignatures (anomalous gases/features on other planets that may indicate life) and technosignatures (evidence of technological activity).
- In those fields, when an anomaly appears scientists do **not** immediately conclude "life" — they seek more data, and anomalies often drive new discoveries.
- The SETI community has deep, decades-long expertise in evaluating whether a candidate signal is real, natural, human technology, or genuinely technological — a methodology directly relevant to UAP triage and to the question of "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence."
- Noted that stigma previously kept technosignatures at arm's length from NASA, but a curiosity-driven agency cannot permanently wall off valid questions; lessons from destigmatizing technosignature research should inform UAP work.
- Argued that if we entertain an extraterrestrial hypothesis at all, then the objects would have had to traverse the solar system — making solar-system observations relevant. The same reasoning that supports searching for extraterrestrial civilizations also makes searching for *artifacts* in our solar system at least plausible.
- As the lead agency for solar-system exploration, NASA could apply existing ground- and space-based NEO/planetary facilities to search for objects with anomalous motion, trajectories, light curves, or spectral signatures; much of the solar system has not been searched for such anomalies.
- Stressed that applying NASA's rigorous astrobiology-style methodology to UAP will teach us something new regardless of the ultimate explanation.

**Notable quotes:**
> "When we discover such an anomaly, we don't conclude that we've discovered life, we seek more data to understand what we're seeing."
> "The galaxy does not stop at the edge of the solar system, and the solar system does not stop at the top of the earth's atmosphere."

## 10. Dr. Karlin Toner — Panel member; aerospace engineer (FAA)

**Key points:**
- Focused on UAP reporting: how to make the "needle" bigger and the "haystack" smaller.
- Identified persistent barriers to reporting: uncertainty about where/how to report; doubt that anyone will act; fear of being shamed or disbelieved; and a widespread view (among scientists and aviators) that UAP research is "fringe," creating major stigma.
- Noted the team had seen only a handful of unclassified UAP images, which generally lacked the contextual metadata needed for scientific analysis, and had heard only one firsthand military-aviator account.
- Recommended NASA: (1) formally assess cultural/social barriers to reporting and studying UAP; (2) leverage its trusted brand to reduce stigma; (3) begin work *internally* on civil-airspace anomaly data sharing, incentivize reporting, assess crowdsourcing, and sponsor/participate in UAP-detection conferences.
- During public Q&A, emphasized that NASA simply holding a public, transparent meeting is itself a first step in normalizing UAP study; that the shift from "UFO" to "UAP" is intentionally more inclusive of all anomalous phenomena; and that interagency collaboration (notably with AARO, FAA, and DOD) is essential.

**Notable quotes:**
> "Many scientists and aviators consider the study of UAPs to be fringe, at best. This suggests there's a significant negative stigma associated with reporting or even researching such phenomena."
> "I think the fact that NASA has called us together here as a panel to look into this, that NASA is hosting a public meeting…that's the first step in trying to really normalize the study of UAPs."

## 11. Josh Semeter — Panel member; Professor of Electrical & Computer Engineering, Boston University (transcript mislabels him "Mike Freie")

**Key points:**
- Walked the panel through a quantitative analysis of the Navy's 2015 "Go Fast" video from the USS Theodore Roosevelt, using data displayed on-screen (camera elevation/azimuth, target range in nautical miles, aircraft altitude, time, indicated airspeed).
- Using basic trigonometry, showed the object is at ~13,000 ft, with the ocean 4.2 miles behind it; apparent high speed is largely a parallax artifact because the sensing jet is moving ~430 mph.
- Using bearing/range at two times ~22 seconds apart, computed object motion of ~390 meters — ~40 mph, consistent with wind speeds at 13,000 ft (e.g., a balloon).
- Stated it is not the panel's task to identify the object, but that this example demonstrates (a) the critical importance of multi-sensor, contextual, quantitative data for deriving physical constraints (altitude, velocity) on UAP, and (b) the cognitive biases and perceptual illusions induced by unfamiliar viewing perspectives.
- Framed this as the core case for why NASA should help standardize how UAP data and reports are captured.

**Notable quotes:**
> "The main point I want to make here is that this multi-sensor approach is absolutely critical to charting a path forward for UAP investigations."
> "This example also serves to illustrate the kind of cognitive bias we have to contend with for UAPs recorded from unfamiliar perspectives."

## 12. Captain Scott Kelly — Panel member; former NASA astronaut and U.S. Navy test pilot

**Key points:**
- Spent 15,000+ flight hours over 30+ years in atmospheric flight and in space; testified that those environments are extremely conducive to optical illusions, explaining why pilots' "Go Fast"-type perceptions are genuine but not necessarily accurate.
- Shared personal anecdotes: an F-14 RIO (radar intercept officer) convinced they had flown past a UFO off Virginia Beach that turned out to be a Bart Simpson balloon; multiple observations from space that initially looked like non-Keplerian objects but were always atmospheric lensing.
- Recounted his brother Senator Mark Kelly's 2008 Shuttle STS-124 incident, where an "unidentified object" in the payload bay (worrisome enough to almost trigger a spacewalk) turned out, on close-up photo, to be the International Space Station 80 miles away — a perspective/distance illusion.
- Stated that sensors suffer the same kinds of perspective/lensing artifacts as human vision, reinforcing the need for multi-source corroboration.

**Notable quotes:**
> "The environment that we fly in, space, or an atmospheric flight, very, very conducive to optical illusions."
> "Every single time when I would look at it long enough, I would realize that it was atmospheric lensing."

## 13. Mike Gold — Panel member; space policy and law expert (Redwire; former NASA associate administrator)

**Key points:**
- Agreed with Semeter/Kelly that multi-source data is essential to draw conclusions; without radar, secondary sightings, and holistic context, single videos are insufficient.
- Commended Administrator Bill Nelson for the courage to have NASA tackle UAP, but warned that ad hoc collation of reports would fail.
- **Called for a permanent, modestly sized NASA UAP office** to serve as the open, civilian, forward-facing counterpart to AARO — tasked with collating/archiving data, supporting reporting, and addressing stigma, so that the panel's recommendations do not "sit on a shelf."
- Pushed back strongly on the "needle in a haystack" framing: expressed frustration that the panel was being asked to monitor phenomena without a defined target signature (radiation? EM? anomalous acceleration?), arguing this is a circular problem.
- Urged NASA to use its brand — via sponsored research, conferences, and symposia — to destigmatize UAP work, noting stigma is not just a cultural problem but a **national-security vulnerability** that rival nations could exploit (cf. the Chinese balloon).

**Notable quotes:**
> "I don't want this to be one of those exercises…I would call for and recommend a permanent office within NASA to support this activity…the open forward-facing counterpart to Sean and AARO."
> "I'm very concerned that this could be effectively done on an ad hoc basis."

## Cross-Cutting Themes

1. **Data quality is the central problem.** Existing UAP data is uncalibrated, fragmented, poorly documented, and heavy with unfiltered background; NASA can bring standards (FAIR principles, multi-sensor curation, open archives).
2. **Stigma is a first-order barrier.** Harassment, ridicule, and the "fringe" label deter pilots, scientists, and citizens from reporting and researching UAP; NASA's credibility is a unique asset for normalization.
3. **Complementarity with AARO.** AARO leads on national security and classified data; NASA leads on open science, civilian/scientific data, and public engagement. A whole-of-government approach is required.
4. **Perceptual and sensor artifacts explain many sightings.** Parallax, gimbal jitter, atmospheric lensing, distance misperception, and filter design create compelling but misleading impressions — as illustrated by the "Go Fast" reanalysis and Scott Kelly's anecdotes.
5. **No evidence of non-human intelligence to date.** The chair and multiple panelists explicitly stated that extraordinary evidence has not been presented, while keeping open the possibility that better data could yield surprises.
6. **Institutionalization.** Multiple panelists (notably Gold) argued for a standing NASA UAP presence (a small permanent office) so the study's recommendations produce durable action rather than another shelf report.
