## 1. Water & Wastewater Capacity Fee Study (Item 23 – First Reading)

**Presenters:**
- **Mike Perry** – Revenue and Finance
- **Murray Hamilton** – Raftelis (consultant that conducted the fee study)
- **Brad Baird** – Deputy Administrator of Infrastructure

**Background**
- The City last adopted water/wastewater capacity fees in **2000** (FY 2020 cycle) and established a mandatory five-year review. This was the first scheduled five-year revisit.
- The City has invested approximately **$3 billion gross** in water and wastewater facilities; nearly 60% of that is system-level (treatment + transmission) capacity.
- Historical grants received (mostly EPA, tied to the Fourth Wastewater Improvement Program) totaled **more than $170 million** and were deducted from the cost basis.
- Future five-year Capital Improvement Program (CIP) for the utility totals **~$1.8 billion**; just under **$80 million** of water-side improvements were included in the fee calculation (wastewater is largely capital-maintenance and excluded from impact-fee recovery).
- System annual revenues exceed **$300 million/year**; the utility holds a **Triple-A bond rating**.
- The Accounting Office processes **~10 million transactions/month**.

**Proposed Fee Changes (per Equivalent Residential Unit / ERU)**

| Component | Current Fee | Calculated Cost | Statutory Cap (FL law) | Recommended Fee |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Water – Systemwide treatment | $1,020 | $1,837 | $1,530 | $1,530 (capped) |
| Water – Transmission (non-CIAC areas; CIAC areas unchanged) | $0 (new) | $985 | (phased) | $985 phased in |
| Wastewater (systemwide single fee) | $1,237 | $1,847 | ~$1,847 (essentially at cap) | $1,847 |

- Florida statute caps impact-fee increases and requires a **four-year phase-in** starting no earlier than **March 1, 2027**.
- Qualified **affordable housing remains exempt** ($0 capacity fee), a policy carried over from 2000.
- Regional comparison: surveyed neighbors average ~**$5,000/ERU** (Orlando ~$4,900; Fort Myers ~$6,700; Fort Lauderdale just under $4,000).

**Projected Revenue Impact**
- By Year 4 (full phase-in, FY 2030), the new fees are projected to generate roughly **$1.5–2.0 million/year** in additional restricted revenue to support the CIP and existing debt service.
- Consultants confirmed the impact on existing ratepayers of *not* adopting the fees would be negligible – roughly **less than $1/month** per customer – because of the system's scale and AAA rating.

**Council Concerns & Debate**
- **Lynn Hurtak** emphasized the gap between the calculated $1,837 water cost and the $1,530 statutory cap: the shortfall falls on existing ratepayers. She urged the public to lobby state legislators to lift impact-fee caps.
- **Bill Carlson** asked for the concrete ratepayer savings of moving forward; staff confirmed it is largely "form over substance" – more a statement that new development pays its share than a material rate reducer.
- **Charlie Miranda** recalled the Pipes Program history: started with a **$2/month** surcharge (water + wastewater, i.e., $2 and $2), now at **$8/$8 per month**, paid cash (not bonded); he warned that without bonding, program costs will outpace collections.
- Public commenter **Stephanie Poynor** argued that the City had left roughly **$1.8 million on the table over the last two years** from capacity-fee under-collection on single-family and multifamily development.
- Public commenter **Michelle Myles** raised concerns about Hillsborough County CPA 25-37 (up to ~4,000 new units in east Hillsborough) potentially drawing on Tampa water/wastewater capacity at City expense.

**Council Action**
- **Motion** (Hurtak) to approve the ordinance on **first reading**; seconded by Miranda. Passed unanimously. A second reading/final adoption is expected at a subsequent meeting, with the new fees not taking effect until developers have had time to incorporate them into rate structures.

## 2. Zion Cemetery Memorial & Preservation Project (Items 16 & 17 – Substitute Resolutions)

**Context**
- Zion Cemetery is a historic African American burial ground in Tampa; approximately **800 souls** (including the great-great-grandmother of Zion Board member Jeraldine Williams) are interred there. The City has been recognized as a national leader among cities rediscovering and honoring lost Black cemeteries.
- Reva Iman (Vice-Chair, Zion Cemetery Maintenance and Preservation Board) and other speakers requested **$8 million** in City funding to complete the memorial, education center, and genealogy center.
- The project is framed not only as a memorial but as an educational/genealogical tourism asset for the City, comparable in significance to other major civic investments. Speakers also thanked Richard Gonzmart / 1905 Family of Restaurants for completing a land swap to help protect the site, and encouraged the City to pursue similar swaps with other landowners (e.g., Patrick Thorpe).

**Council Action**
- **Motion** (Carlson), seconded by Maniscalco, to approve Items 16 & 17 (substitute resolutions). Passed on a voice vote. Final funding level to be determined through subsequent budget process.

## 3. Rome Yard / West Riverwalk – Related Urban Affordable Housing Development (Items 14 & 15)

**Presenter:** Albert Milo, President, Related Urban; Rebecca Johns (Legal).

**Project Status & Financing**
- Development is split between a North Parcel (environmental challenges, now cleaned) and South Parcel (to be bifurcated by the Riverwalk extension).
- **Phase 4** (the phase before Council) is a **$94 million project** delivering **230 additional affordable housing units**; fully financed including **over $3 million** of Related Urban's own equity and state funding; about to close once permits issue.
- Developer's prior commitments include:
  - **$2.5 million** investment in Phase 1 (part of which, ~$500K+, went to assist the homeless-services site)
  - Another **$3 million** developer investment in a subsequent phase
  - **40% small/minority business commitment** on Phase 3A – reported as exceeded at **52%**
  - **40% local-hire commitment** – reported at **43%**
  - 3,000+ sq. ft. Workforce Training Center (Phase 3A, under construction; building is up to the 8th floor)
  - 5 live-work units (Phase 3A)
  - Overall unit count cited: 464 units total across phases, **>80% affordable** (20%–120% AMI), with 50 market-rate units in Phase 3A and 267 more in later phases.
- Townhome parcels are being sold to the Related Group for **$2,500 each** per the original contract (criticized in public comment); buyers can resell at up to **200% AMI (~$200,000/year income)**.
- The developer is the **only RFP responder that did not request City subsidy** for the project.

**The Extension Request**
- Phase 4 completion date: original **December 2027**, extended to **September 2028**. This is the first extension for Phase 4 specifically (earlier extensions covered other phasing/timing plans).
- Earlier environmental due diligence on the North Parcel took over 1.5 years; contract originally called for 180 days – today is **1,436 days** since contract approval (per public comment).
- Phases 1 and 2 of the five total phases have not yet started; staff acknowledged future phases will likely require similar time extensions as financing closes phase-by-phase.

**Council & Public Concerns**
- Multiple members (Clendenin, Carlson, Hurtak) raised concerns about repeated extensions, perceived preferential treatment, alleged conflicts involving a former mayor and the mayor's partner's firm lobbying for the developer, and lack of lobbyist registration.
- The delays are stalling adjacent public projects, including a portion of the **Riverwalk** that Related is contractually responsible for delivering (originally slated for January 2026).
- Public commenter Stephanie Poynor called for the City to cancel the contract for nonperformance and criticized the $2,500 townhome pricing.
- Naya Young requested: (1) a list of minority/women-owned contractors and (2) enhanced progress reporting; staff committed to amend the lease to require **quarterly reports covering both community benefits and overall construction**.

**Council Action**
- Motion (Maniscalco), seconded (Viera), **to approve Items 14 & 15 with the added quarterly-report condition**.
- Roll-call vote: **5–2 in favor**. Yes: Miranda, Maniscalco, Hurtak ("begrudgingly"), Young, Viera. No: **Bill Carlson, Alan Clendenin**.

## 4. Howard Avenue Annex / TPD Forensics Lab & Impound Facility (Items 26, 27, 28)

**Presenters:** Patrick Messmer (TPD); Mike Perry (Revenue & Finance).

**Project Cost (revised Sources & Uses)**
- Original project (previously called the Impound Lot project) was budgeted at **$40 million**.
- Through value engineering the project was **reduced by $14 million**.
- Funds already spent on the earlier impound-lot phase: **$7 million**, achieved by reducing current bond proceeds by **$175,000** and future bond proceeds by about **$1.3 million**.
- Phase 2 (Howard Avenue Annex / TPD Forensics Lab) is now budgeted at **$27.2 million**.
- **Total current project cost: $34.2 million** (confirmed by Mike Perry and consistent with Budget Analyst Hagar Kopesky's memo).
- Potential **additional savings up to ~$2 million** may be realized through contracted contingency, T&I equipment, specialty equipment, and owner contingency (estimate, not guaranteed).
- Funding: part of the $7M already bonded; most of the $27.2M Phase 2 is expected to be bonded (CIT funds may not arrive in time).

**Towing / Impound Cost Savings**
- Under the current Crockett's Towing contract (in place since July 2023), the City pays a flat **$10,000/month for unlimited tows** plus evidence storage at the Crockett facility.
- Historical tow costs (pre-Crockett rotation wrecker system) averaged about **$8,300/month**.
- With the new on-site TPD impound/annex facility, TPD will rebid the towing contract; the City will no longer rent or staff the Crockett facility or store evidence there. TPD anticipates "substantial" savings – the new tow rate is expected to be "nowhere close to $10,000/month."
- The Crockett contract is a five-year deal (Drew Park location); the City will solicit other vendors when appropriate.

**Procedural Note**
- City Attorney Martin Shelby flagged that Council rules require contracts **over $20 million** to appear on the agenda twice. Because Items 26–28 were heard only once on April 2, Council **rescinded its initial approval** and set the items for **second reading on April 16, 2026**.

**Council Action (initial, before rescission)**
- Motion (Maniscalco), seconded (Viera) – passed unanimously.
- Immediately **reconsidered and re-scheduled for second reading April 16** after the procedural issue was raised; all members supported placing it on the April 16 agenda.
- Clendenin stated he intends to support the project at second reading because of the significant value engineering that reduced the cost. Hurtak noted she will continue to scrutinize contingencies for further savings.

## 6. Florida Gas Transmission Settlement – Park Funding Reallocation (New Business)

**Introduced by:** Bill Carlson

**Item**
- In **FY 2025**, the City received a **$650,000 settlement** from Florida Gas Transmission Company, LLC as compensation for a natural-gas pipeline easement on properties in the Port Tampa area.
- The funds were deposited into **Project 1002116 (Small Neighborhood Parks)**, a citywide capital project.
- Carlson requested a financial resolution reallocating the **$650,000** to **Project 1002293 (Gandy Park South & A.J. Palonis Park)**, because the GMP Council recently approved for that park does not fund all originally planned amenities. Because the compensation is for public land lost in District 4, he argued the money should be directed back to benefit that area.

**Council Action**
- Chairman Clendenin requested additional information; Miranda asked staff to verify what is missing and provide all facts.
- **Placed on Unfinished Business for the April 16, 2026 meeting** for staff report and further consideration.

**Top-line takeaways**
- The Council advanced two of the largest capital items on the agenda – the **$34.2 million TPD Howard Avenue Annex** (sent to second reading April 16 after a procedural correction) and the **$94 million Rome Yard Phase 4** (approved 5–2 with conditions) – while showing clear appetite for continued cost scrutiny, especially through contingencies and future bonding decisions.
- The **water/wastewater capacity-fee update** passed first reading unanimously and will generate an estimated **$1.5–2.0 million/year** of restricted revenue once fully phased in by March 2030, but State-mandated caps will continue shifting a portion of growth costs onto existing ratepayers.
- **$650,000** in Florida Gas Transmission settlement money and **$8 million** for Zion Cemetery are the two most significant unresolved appropriations carried into future meetings.
- Council began formal scheduling of the **FY 2027 budget workshops** for August 3, 10, and 17, 2026.
