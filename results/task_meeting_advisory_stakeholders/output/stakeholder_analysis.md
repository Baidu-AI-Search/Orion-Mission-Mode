# Stakeholder Analysis: CSMAC Meeting – 1755–1850 MHz Spectrum Sharing
**Meeting:** Commerce Spectrum Management Advisory Committee (CSMAC), May 30, 2012
**Subject:** Repurposing the 1755–1850 MHz federal band for commercial wireless broadband via sharing and/or relocation
**Author:** Analysis of public meeting transcript

---

## 2. Areas of Agreement Across Stakeholders

1. **The 1755–1850 MHz band is the right priority.** It is the last major sub‑3 GHz piece that doesn't force relocation of major radar or radionavigation systems, and sub‑3 GHz is essential for commercial wireless coverage economics.
2. **The old "vacate and auction" model is ending.** Multiple speakers (Strickling, Nebbia, Power) agreed that 25‑MHz‑by‑25‑MHz clearing cannot reach the 500 MHz target, and that the era of fully vacating federal spectrum is drawing to a close.
3. **500 MHz in 10 years requires sharing.** Government and commercial speakers alike accepted that sharing (in some combination of exclusion zones, small cells, dynamic/cognitive access, and phased relocation) is a necessary policy tool, not an ideological preference.
4. **Industry and government must collaborate in the same room.** There was universal skepticism that NTIA or agencies could produce an acceptable answer alone, and universal acceptance of the CSMAC working-group model co-chaired by government + industry.
5. **The 5 GHz Wi-Fi effort is the right precedent.** Members repeatedly cited 5 GHz unlicensed sharing as a successful model of joint government–industry technical problem-solving.
6. **The 1695–1710 MHz weather-satellite band is the low-hanging fruit.** All sides agreed it can be resolved more quickly (September target) via refined exclusion areas.
7. **Full relocation is technically possible but impractical on a 10-year, $18B basis.** Even carriers did not argue for 100% clearing at that price/timeline.
8. **A phased approach is acceptable.** The lower 25 MHz (1755–1780) can potentially be made available earlier as part of a longer-term plan, possibly in time to pair with 2155–2180 MHz.
9. **Law-enforcement surveillance is the hardest sub-problem** (echoing 1710–1755 experience).

## 3. Key Tensions and Conflicts

| # | Tension | Between | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Sharing vs. Clearing/Relocation** | Carriers (prefer cleared, exclusive spectrum) vs. NTIA/DoD/OSTP (prefer sharing to control cost/timeline) | Carriers want certainty and contiguous spectrum; agencies argue that if sharing works for 10 years, there's no reason to spend $18B to move. Povelites explicitly pushed back to keep relocation *on the table* and to validate the $18B estimate. |
| 2 | **Commercial architecture assumptions (macro vs. small-cell / PCAST vs. carrier model)** | Carriers & some WG pragmatists (Tramont, Kahn) vs. public-interest/tech advocates (Calabrese), Rush, Obuchowski | Calabrese and Rush argued that small-cell/lower-power scenarios open far more sharing potential; Obuchowski warned that PCAST and the carriers have fundamentally different worldviews with very different auction-revenue implications, and the FCC has not chosen. Kahn thought standards (LTE family) already constrained the solution space enough. |
| 3 | **Common commercial parameters up-front vs. let each WG develop them** | Rush, Calabrese, Warren (Lockheed), Obuchowski (pro upfront common model) vs. Tramont, Nebbia, Kahn (pro parallel WGs with cross-communication) | Rush wanted an INT‑2000-style industry parameter submission *before* WGs launch; Tramont argued a uniform agreement is unachievable and better fixed through consistent representation; Nebbia deferred the question but acknowledged WG outputs must be consistent. |
| 4 | **Information asymmetry / classification vs. proprietary IP** | Federal agencies (classified/sensitive) vs. commercial operators (proprietary network plans) | Power flagged this explicitly; Nebbia noted that classified info usually isn't needed but sensitive law-enforcement details may still be a friction point. Gibson asked how classified concerns would be surfaced in-process. |
| 5 | **Transition sequencing for law-enforcement (city-by-city)** | Law enforcement / DOJ/DHS vs. carriers ("NFL city" priorities) | Last relocation saw conflict when government transition order didn't match where carriers wanted to build first. Both sides want to align the order, but priorities will differ. |
| 6 | **Regulatory construct for satellite & EW (shared, but with guarantees)** | DoD (needs training/operational guarantees) vs. industry (needs interference protection *into* commercial networks from satellite uplinks) | Satellite case is unusual: interference runs *from* federal uplinks *into* commercial networks, requiring a bi-directional regulatory framework. EW training needs "we can jam/train when we need to" carve-outs. |
| 7 | **Process transparency / public participation** | Public participant (Mr. Snider) vs. CSMAC chair | Snider alleged a pattern of shrinking public comment; Fontes defended this meeting as informational only and committed to future public comment when reports are out, but tension about openness remained. |
| 8 | **Cost-estimate credibility** | Carriers (Povelites asked for re-validation of $18B) vs. NTIA (says formal CSEA process firms up costs later) | NTIA's own history shows estimates can drift (e.g., 2001 estimate of ~$900M became ~$1.5B actual); carriers want costs stress-tested *before* a sharing/relocation decision is locked. |

## 4. Unresolved Questions Raised During the Meeting

1. **Who does the sharing analysis, and under what assumptions?** Charlie Rush's opening question — will NTIA, the WGs, or industry perform the interference analyses? Answered only with "cooperative effort," not a clear analytic owner.
2. **What is the agreed common set of commercial-system parameters (power, cell size, architecture, loading)?** No mechanism was set at the meeting; Rush volunteered to work on it but did not get a formal mandate.
3. **Will the FCC adopt a macro-cellular or a small-cell / PCAST-style sharing model?** As Janice Obuchowski noted, this fundamentally changes the value and feasibility of any consensus CSMAC produces, and neither the FCC nor Congress had signaled a direction.
4. **How accurate is the $18 billion relocation cost estimate?** Carl Povelites directly asked if the cost estimates would be re-examined; Nebbia deferred this to the later CSEA process.
5. **How can sensitive/classified information be handled without derailing open, multi-stakeholder dialogue?** Mark Gibson raised this; Nebbia hoped classified material would not be needed but offered no procedure if it is.
6. **How will the five working groups coordinate to avoid inconsistent assumptions?** No standing cross-WG mechanism was established; the question was effectively tabled for future meetings.
7. **What is the transition order for law-enforcement/surveillance clearance, and how will it align with carrier build-out priorities ("NFL cities" vs. government sequence)?** Acknowledged as the biggest practical hurdle but not solved.
8. **What regulatory construct can protect DoD EW training and satellite uplinks while still giving commercial operators enforceable interference protections?** Flagged as the critical design question for WG 3 but no direction set.
9. **How will airborne systems (UAVs, telemetry, PGMs, air-combat training) be measured and modeled?** NTIA noted that T-Mobile/CTIA had filed an STA to perform measurements, but results were pending; airborne sharing feasibility remains open.
10. **Will the lower 25 MHz (1755–1780) be cleared/auctioned early in time to pair with 2155–2180 MHz for the January target?** Framed as a goal but contingent on WG outcomes.
11. **What happens to the pre-existing CSMAC subcommittees (500 MHz, Sharing Improvements, Spectrum Management Improvements)?** Put on partial hiatus; Tramont proposed co-chairs come back in 10 days with a wrap-up plan, but no decision taken.
12. **Will public comment remain a regular part of future CSMAC meetings?** Chair Fontes committed to it but provided no written policy.
13. **Can the process deliver *consensus* reports (as Nebbia insisted) given the diversity of views, or will unresolved issues bubble up?** Process goal stated, but not reconciled with the breadth of unresolved technical and policy questions above.

---

## 5. Summary Assessment

The May 30, 2012 CSMAC meeting marked the operational launch of one of the most consequential federal–commercial spectrum-sharing efforts in U.S. history. Stakeholders broadly agreed on the **goal** (free up federal spectrum in the 1755–1850 MHz band for wireless broadband) and on the **process** (five co-chaired working groups producing consensus recommendations to NTIA). However, the meeting exposed a fundamental fault line: **carriers want cleared, exclusive spectrum on a predictable timeline, while federal agencies (led by DoD) and the White House see sharing as the only financially and operationally realistic path to 500 MHz in 10 years.** Underneath that headline tension lie unresolved technical questions about commercial network architecture, parameter standardization, the treatment of classified/proprietary information, and the sequencing of law-enforcement relocation — all of which the working groups were expected to resolve between mid-2012 and January 2013.
