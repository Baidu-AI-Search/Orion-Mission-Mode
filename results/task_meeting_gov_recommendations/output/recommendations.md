# NASA Public UAP Meeting – Extracted Recommendations

This document compiles all actionable recommendations made by panelists, presenters, and guests during NASA's first public meeting on Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena (UAPs/UFOs), grouped by category and listed in order of appearance in the transcript. Each entry includes the recommendation, the speaker who proposed it, the context/rationale, and a category classification.

---

## Data Collection

1. **Recommendation:** Characterize known background events (aircraft, drones, balloons, ionospheric phenomena, etc.) first so that anomalous outliers can be reliably identified.
   - **Who proposed it:** David Spergel (Panel Chair)
   - **Context:** Opening remarks noting that current UAP data efforts are unsystematic and fragmented, and that a well-understood "normal" background is a prerequisite for anomaly detection.
   - **Category:** Data Collection

2. **Recommendation:** Survey existing commercial, civil, NASA, NOAA, FAA, and other sensor data to determine which existing sensors can actually detect objects matching the typical UAP target profile.
   - **Who proposed it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (Director, AARO)
   - **Context:** As a first step in AARO's scientific plan, understand which existing earth-sensing satellites, airborne platforms, and ground radars are relevant before building new ones.
   - **Category:** Data Collection

3. **Recommendation:** Deploy purpose-built, dedicated sensors in select locations to detect, track, and characterize UAP targets.
   - **Who proposed it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
   - **Context:** After determining which existing sensors are relevant, augment with custom-built sensors placed in select areas for targeted surveillance.
   - **Category:** Data Collection

4. **Recommendation:** Share tip-and-cue alerts coming from pilot reports with the scientific and academic community so that additional collection can be tasked the same way it is for the military/intelligence community.
   - **Who proposed it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
   - **Context:** When a pilot reports an event, DOD/IC can task new collection; the same tip-and-cue flow should be opened up to academic scientists.
   - **Category:** Data Collection

5. **Recommendation:** Apply AI/ML tools to search archived calibrated scientific data from instruments worldwide for anomalous signatures that correlate with reported UAP events.
   - **Who proposed it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
   - **Context:** Large volumes of calibrated scientific data already exist and are a low-cost source for mining potential UAP correlations.
   - **Category:** Data Collection

6. **Recommendation:** Compare distributions of unclassified/civilian UAP sightings with classified national-security sighting maps to build a holistic picture.
   - **Who proposed it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
   - **Context:** Described as "low-hanging fruit": overlaying public and classified sighting distributions yields a more complete geographic picture of events.
   - **Category:** Data Collection

7. **Recommendation:** Build a robust catalog (roadmap) of existing and potential scientific data sources — NASA, NOAA, FAA, commercial, nonprofit — that could be synthesized to study UAP.
   - **Who proposed it:** Karlin Toner (Panelist)
   - **Context:** Panel discussion of the eight charge questions; the panel had identified sources but not yet done a robust cataloging effort, which was proposed as a modest first step.
   - **Category:** Data Collection

8. **Recommendation:** NASA should serve as a civilian clearinghouse for cross-government civilian UAP-relevant data sets, including FAA radar/tracking data.
   - **Who proposed it:** David Spergel (Panel Chair)
   - **Context:** NASA already has experience acting as a clearinghouse for multi-agency civilian data in other domains; FAA radar data was specifically called out as an underused dataset.
   - **Category:** Data Collection

9. **Recommendation:** Conduct consistent, long-term (persistent) time-series observations of the sky to establish what is "normal" so anomalies can be detected.
   - **Who proposed it:** Dr. Paula Bontempi (Panelist), seconded by Nicola Fox
   - **Context:** The panel noted that long-term, consistent observations are unglamorous but essential — you cannot identify the anomalous without a baseline of the ordinary.
   - **Category:** Data Collection

10. **Recommendation:** Use a multi-sensor, multi-platform, multi-site approach for new data collection, capturing images, sound, temperature, spectral data, and motion tracking simultaneously.
    - **Who proposed it:** Federica Bianco (Panelist)
    - **Context:** Multi-sensor corroboration (as in Navy aviator cases) is the only way to derive altitude, velocity, and other physical parameters and avoid single-perspective illusions.
    - **Category:** Data Collection

11. **Recommendation:** Collect rich metadata alongside every observation: sensor type/brand, noise characteristics, acquisition time, instrument sensitivity, location, temperature, and environmental conditions.
    - **Who proposed it:** Federica Bianco (Panelist)
    - **Context:** Metadata is essential for calibration, cross-referencing, and enabling automated/ML analysis; current UAP reports largely lack it.
    - **Category:** Data Collection

12. **Recommendation:** Leverage existing professional-grade infrastructure — astrophysical/geographical observatories (ground and satellite) and facilities built for near-Earth object/meteor detection, including time-domain surveys — for UAP study.
    - **Who proposed it:** Federica Bianco (Panelist)
    - **Context:** Modern observatories designed for time-domain anomalies and fast-moving objects already have many of the characteristics needed for UAP detection.
    - **Category:** Data Collection

13. **Recommendation:** Extend NASA's search to solar-system and space-based anomalies, using existing planetary-defense and solar-system missions to look for objects with anomalous motion, trajectories, light curves, or spectra.
    - **Who proposed it:** David Grinspoon (Panelist); echoed by David Spergel
    - **Context:** Beyond airspace, NASA has responsibility for deep space; most of the solar system has not been searched for artifacts or anomalies, and existing/planned missions could support modest data-analysis efforts.
    - **Category:** Data Collection

14. **Recommendation:** NASA should look internally at how civil anomaly data is shared, and how existing air-traffic management data acquisition systems can be modified to capture additional UAP-relevant data for both past and future events.
    - **Who proposed it:** Karlin Toner (Panelist); reinforced by Nicola Fox
    - **Context:** Existing reporting protocols and ATM systems can be adapted/tuned (sensor tuning, multimodal spectrum collection, time/geostamping) to better capture future UAP events.
    - **Category:** Data Collection

15. **Recommendation:** Establish persistent future air-traffic management enhancements for UAP data capture: tuning sensor platforms, multimodal spectrum collection, and time/geostamping to corroborate sightings.
    - **Who proposed it:** Nicola Fox (Panel facilitator, summarizing panel discussion)
    - **Context:** As part of answering charge question 8, these specific enhancements were offered as concrete ways to acquire future UAP data.
    - **Category:** Data Collection

## Data Standards

16. **Recommendation:** Bring UAP data into compliance with the FAIR principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) so that scientific and machine-learning analysis can be applied.
    - **Who proposed it:** Federica Bianco (Panelist)
    - **Context:** Current UAP data is inconsistent, inhomogeneous, uncalibrated, poorly documented, incomplete, and not systematically retrievable; it fails the FAIR standards required for rigorous science.
    - **Category:** Data Standards

17. **Recommendation:** Establish organized, centralized data repositories to enable automated retrieval of data and metadata as a prerequisite for systematic, AI-ready UAP science.
    - **Who proposed it:** Federica Bianco (Panelist)
    - **Context:** Without curated repositories, retrieving data for even a single event remains entirely person-powered and cannot be automated for ML.
    - **Category:** Data Standards

18. **Recommendation:** Use well-calibrated, well-characterized sensors (and well-characterized detector/camera systems) with cross-calibration across different detector types rather than insisting on a single uniform detector.
    - **Who proposed it:** David Spergel and Walter Scott (Panel discussion)
    - **Context:** Anomaly/outlier searches are sensitive to detector-specific artifacts (e.g., optical ghosting); detectors must be well characterized and cross-calibrated so results can be combined.
    - **Category:** Data Standards

19. **Recommendation:** Develop a more specific, working definition of what "anomalous" means for NASA's study (beyond "not readily understandable to the operator or sensor").
    - **Who proposed it:** Dr. Nadia Drake (Panelist)
    - **Context:** AARO's definition was viewed as too vague; a more precise definition is needed to bound the search space and align data collection/citizen-science efforts.
    - **Category:** Data Standards

20. **Recommendation:** Adapt lessons from biosignature and technosignature/SETI research — where standardized datasets and mature ML/AI workflows already exist — to standardize UAP data and analysis.
    - **Who proposed it:** Speaker 5 (Panelist); affirmed by David Grinspoon
    - **Context:** Those fields have established community standards for data and analysis from which the UAP field should learn, even while acknowledging technosignatures is itself relatively young.
    - **Category:** Data Standards

21. **Recommendation:** Document and communicate sensor thresholds and resolution bounds for every dataset so analysts know the physical limits of the systems they are using.
    - **Who proposed it:** Nicola Fox (summarizing panel)
    - **Context:** Scientists need explicit knowledge of what each sensor can and cannot see to interpret anomalies correctly.
    - **Category:** Data Standards

## Sensor Technology

22. **Recommendation:** Investigate whether the FAA can collect and store raw (unfiltered) radar/sensor data, or enable post-hoc calibration, so that phenomena filtered out by current optimization settings can be studied.
    - **Who proposed it:** Reggie Brothers (Panelist), posed to Mike Freie (FAA)
    - **Context:** FAA filters remove non-aircraft returns (birds, insects, clutter) to give clean controller displays; preserving raw data could capture UAP phenomena lost to filtering. FAA noted feasibility but cited cost and technical challenges.
    - **Category:** Sensor Technology

23. **Recommendation:** Build a crowdsourcing platform (not just guidelines) — including an open-source mobile application — that collects both data and metadata from citizen scientists' phones, centralizes it, and supports coordinated follow-up.
    - **Who proposed it:** Federica Bianco (Panelist), in response to Walter Scott
    - **Context:** Best-practice guidelines alone are insufficient. A platform is needed so a sighting by one observer can trigger a networked, multi-sensor follow-up by other citizens and transmit data/metadata to a centralized, curated hub.
    - **Category:** Sensor Technology

## Partnerships

24. **Recommendation:** NASA should be a strong, open partner to AARO, complementing (not replicating) DOD's work.
    - **Who proposed it:** David Spergel (Panel Chair)
    - **Context:** NASA's civilian, open-science role is complementary to DOD/AARO's national-security mission; close partnership was described as essential.
    - **Category:** Partnerships

25. **Recommendation:** Establish academic partnerships to explore what physical signatures should be expected from objects exhibiting anomalous motion/behavior, and use that to tune collection architecture.
    - **Who proposed it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
    - **Context:** Academia can help map "what signatures should look like" so sensors and collection can be tuned appropriately.
    - **Category:** Partnerships

26. **Recommendation:** Partner with DOD and DOE national labs to explore the fundamental physics of current and historical UAP observations, and peer-review advanced (not-yet-engineered) capabilities in the open literature.
    - **Who proposed it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
    - **Context:** Fundamental-physics exploration, parameterization, and peer-reviewed publication are needed so hypotheses can be matched against data to eliminate alternatives.
    - **Category:** Partnerships

27. **Recommendation:** Expand foreign partnerships beyond military/intelligence channels into a robust international scientific/academic community of interest that reviews data, capabilities, and conducts joint analysis.
    - **Who proposed it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
    - **Context:** Existing military/intel collaborations abroad should be broadened into the open scientific community for global data sharing and analysis.
    - **Category:** Partnerships

28. **Recommendation:** Bring together interdisciplinary scientific communities that do not normally collaborate (e.g., astrobiologists/SETI researchers with Earth scientists, astronomers, oceanographers, biological and physical scientists) to establish baselines of "normal" and help explain UAP reports.
    - **Who proposed it:** Dr. Paula Bontempi and David Grinspoon (Panelists)
    - **Context:** New insights often come from crossing disciplinary boundaries; terrestrial analogs and Earth-science baselines are essential context for anomaly detection.
    - **Category:** Partnerships

29. **Recommendation:** Leverage NASA's unique strength in public/private partnerships and longstanding international-collaboration record to obtain data and co-develop observation technologies.
    - **Who proposed it:** Dr. Paula Bontempi (Panelist)
    - **Context:** Partners include other federal agencies (FAA, NOAA), private/commercial satellite operators, and international space agencies — all of which may hold relevant data.
    - **Category:** Partnerships

## Public Engagement

30. **Recommendation:** Destigmatize UAP reporting and research; NASA should actively work to reduce cultural and social barriers for both civilian and professional reporters.
    - **Who proposed it:** David Spergel (opening); reiterated by Dr. Paula Bontempi, Karlin Toner, David Grinspoon, and Dan Evans
    - **Context:** Stigma causes commercial pilots, scientists, and others not to report events; Dan Evans noted panelists themselves have faced online abuse. NASA's trusted brand is uniquely positioned to counter this.
    - **Category:** Public Engagement

31. **Recommendation:** More fully assess cultural and social barriers to studying and reporting UAP, and implement a plan that leverages NASA's brand image to remove these obstacles.
    - **Who proposed it:** Karlin Toner (Panelist)
    - **Context:** Formal recommendation to fellow panelists that NASA sponsor an internal effort to study civil anomaly data-sharing practices, incentivize reporting, evaluate crowdsourcing, and sponsor/participate in UAP conferences.
    - **Category:** Public Engagement

32. **Recommendation:** Use UAP as an opportunity to educate the public about the scientific method — including skepticism, hypothesis-driven inquiry, calibration, independent corroboration, and peer review.
    - **Who proposed it:** Dr. Paula Bontempi and David Spergel (Panelists)
    - **Context:** Because UAPs command intense public interest, they provide a high-visibility platform to demonstrate open, rigorous science in action.
    - **Category:** Public Engagement

33. **Recommendation:** Communicate findings openly and transparently, using NASA's tradition of public trust to report results (even mundane ones) and set realistic expectations about evidence thresholds.
    - **Who proposed it:** Dr. Paula Bontempi and David Spergel (Panelists)
    - **Context:** Extraordinary claims (e.g., non-human intelligence) require extraordinary evidence; open communication of what is and isn't known is essential to maintain trust.
    - **Category:** Public Engagement

## Scientific Method

34. **Recommendation:** Pursue hypothesis-driven scientific inquiry starting from known hotspots and known conditions, using available data to untangle the "chicken-and-egg" problem of not knowing exactly what phenomenon is being sought.
    - **Who proposed it:** Nicola Fox
    - **Context:** Even though the target phenomenon is poorly defined, known hotspots from FAA/AARO data give concrete starting points for hypothesis-driven study.
    - **Category:** Scientific Method

35. **Recommendation:** Apply rigorous peer review, physical testing, and modeling/simulation to validate UAP analyses before reaching conclusions; publish in peer-reviewed journals.
    - **Who proposed it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO)
    - **Context:** For objects with potentially anomalous traits, AARO uses physical testing, modeling/simulation, and peer review; NASA and the academic community should do likewise.
    - **Category:** Scientific Method

36. **Recommendation:** Prioritize multi-sensor corroboration and quantitative analysis (e.g., deriving altitude and velocity from ranging + infrared) over eyewitness-only reports; treat eyewitness reports as contextual (locations/seasonality) but pair them with sensor data and biophysical/psychophysical witness assessments.
    - **Who proposed it:** Josh (panel presenter, Speaker 3) and Federica Bianco
    - **Context:** The "Go Fast" video and other cases demonstrate how multi-sensor, quantitative analysis resolves apparent anomalies (e.g., parallax, wind-speed objects at 13,000 ft) that eyewitnesses misperceive.
    - **Category:** Scientific Method

37. **Recommendation:** Establish a rapid follow-up capability so that when an anomaly is detected, additional observations can be promptly tasked — analogous to time-domain astrophysics follow-up networks.
    - **Who proposed it:** Federica Bianco (Panelist)
    - **Context:** UAP are often ephemeral; without rapid response, the chance to collect critical follow-up data is lost.
    - **Category:** Scientific Method

38. **Recommendation:** Use AI/ML — including NLP on pilot reports, computer-vision/target recognition, and unsupervised anomaly detection — as analysis tools, but only after data standards are in place; be cautious with synthetic training data because it can embed biases.
    - **Who proposed it:** Sean Kirkpatrick (AARO), Federica Bianco, Speaker 5, and David Spergel
    - **Context:** Kirkpatrick is researching NLP for pilot reports and target-recognition models; Bianco warned synthetic data can encode our assumptions about anomalies; Speaker 5 noted AI can also help design what data to collect.
    - **Category:** Scientific Method

39. **Recommendation:** Apply the same rigorous methodology NASA uses to study possible life elsewhere (astrobiology/SETI/technosignatures) to UAP — search for anomalies, then seek more data rather than jumping to extraordinary conclusions.
    - **Who proposed it:** David Grinspoon (Panelist)
    - **Context:** Astrobiology/SETI have mature protocols for evaluating candidate biosignatures/technosignatures against mundane explanations; those methods should be applied to UAP.
    - **Category:** Scientific Method

## Other

40. **Recommendation:** Create a permanent (likely modest-sized) office within NASA to collate UAP reporting and data, archive information, and act as the open, civilian-facing counterpart to AARO so that the panel's work does not end up "sitting on a shelf."
    - **Who proposed it:** Mike Gold (Panelist)
    - **Context:** Mike Gold expressed concern that ad hoc panels often produce unused reports and called for a sustained home for UAP work within NASA to drive stigma reduction, data collation, and archiving.
    - **Category:** Other

41. **Recommendation:** Frame NASA's UAP effort as a long-term, sustained program of study, recognizing that the work will take time and is consistent with NASA's history of long-running scientific missions.
    - **Who proposed it:** Dr. Paula Bontempi; echoed by Dan Evans on the federal budget process
    - **Context:** UAP study will not be resolved quickly; NASA should plan for extensive, ongoing investigation, with funding decisions following the independent team's final recommendations through the normal federal budget process.
    - **Category:** Other

42. **Recommendation:** Continue to focus near-term recommendations on the aerial domain (where the preponderance of sightings occur) while recognizing the expanded scope of "Anomalous" (which includes space, ocean, etc.).
    - **Who proposed it:** Panel consensus (Nadia Drake, Shelley Wright, Dan Evans, David Spergel)
    - **Context:** The shift from "Aerial" to "Anomalous" expanded the panel's scope, but the group agreed the aerial domain remains the priority for recommendations, with solar-system/space work as a complementary NASA role.
    - **Category:** Other

## Summary of Most Emphasized / Repeated Recommendations

Across the meeting, several themes recurred so frequently that they rose to the level of consensus recommendations:

- **High-quality, well-calibrated data is the central need.** Virtually every speaker — from David Spergel's opening ("we need high quality data with well calibrated instruments") to Nicola Fox's closing charge questions — returned to the point that existing UAP data are fragmented, uncalibrated, poorly curated, and insufficient for scientific conclusions. Targeted, multi-sensor, multi-modal data collection with rich metadata was the single most repeated theme.
- **Data standardization and a centralized, FAIR-compliant repository.** Federica Bianco's presentation crystallized a cross-panel view: without organized repositories that meet FAIR standards (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable), neither traditional analysis nor AI/ML can be applied at scale.
- **Reducing stigma and improving reporting.** Stigma was identified as a primary reason events go unreported (especially by commercial pilots and scientists). Multiple panelists — Spergel, Bontempi, Toner, Grinspoon, and Gold — argued that NASA's trusted brand and public-facing role give it a unique responsibility to destigmatize reporting, and Karlin Toner explicitly recommended a NASA plan to address cultural and social barriers.
- **A strong, complementary partnership with AARO, plus broader academic, international, interagency, and private-sector collaboration.** NASA was repeatedly described as the open, civilian counterpart to AARO's defense/intelligence mission; panelists urged NASA to build bridges across federal agencies (FAA, NOAA), national labs, academia, international partners, and commercial satellite operators, and to convene interdisciplinary scientific communities that do not normally collaborate.
- **Leveraging AI/ML and citizen science — but on top of proper data foundations.** There was broad enthusiasm for applying AI, machine learning, NLP of pilot reports, and crowdsourced mobile sensing to UAP detection, tempered by consistent warnings that these tools cannot be productive until data standards, calibration, and curated repositories are in place. Synthetic training data was flagged as risky due to bias.
- **A permanent NASA UAP home.** Mike Gold's call for a modest permanent office within NASA — to collate data, archive findings, serve as the open counterpart to AARO, and ensure the panel's work does not "sit on a shelf" — drew explicit support as the institutional mechanism for translating the study into ongoing action.
- **Rigorous, hypothesis-driven, multi-sensor science.** The panel repeatedly emphasized that eyewitness reports alone are insufficient, that extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence, and that corroborating observations across multiple well-characterized sensors is essential to avoid the parallax, optical-illusion, and sensor-artifact pitfalls illustrated by the "Go Fast" video and other cases.

In short, the panel's overarching message was: **NASA's most valuable contribution will be to bring open-science rigor — calibrated instruments, curated data, peer review, interdisciplinary collaboration, and public trust — to a field that has historically suffered from fragmented data, stigma, and sensationalism.**
