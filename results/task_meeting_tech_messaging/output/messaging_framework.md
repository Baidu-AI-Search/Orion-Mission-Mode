# GitLab Messaging Framework — Collaborative Exercise
**Source:** Product Marketing Weekly Meeting, 2021-06-28
**Context:** Toward the end of the meeting, the team ran a quick collaborative messaging session to draft punchy taglines for a three-pillar messaging framework. The deliverable was due end-of-day.

---

## 1. The Three Messaging Pillars

The team worked on three pillars (the "boxes" in the messaging framework deck). They correspond to the three core value propositions GitLab wanted to land:

| # | Pillar Theme | Core Idea |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Visibility / Transparency / Single Source of Truth** | Collaboration, shared truth, transparency across the org |
| 2 | **End-to-End Platform Control** | One comprehensive DevSecOps platform — a single "software factory" under your control |
| 3 | **Speed + Security / Velocity with Confidence** | Ship fast without sacrificing security, quality, or control (the "no trade-off" promise) |

---

## 2. All Candidate Taglines/Phrases, Grouped by Pillar

### Pillar 1 — Visibility / Single Source of Truth
- *"From road map to company vision, we are transparent"* — starter/fodder phrase provided with the framework
- *"Collaborate on everything"* — half of the starter phrase *"Automate nearly anything, collaborate on everything"*
- ✅ **"A single source of truth, countless possibilities"** — William's draft; the team's clear favorite

### Pillar 2 — End-to-End Control / Comprehensive Platform
- *"Automate nearly anything"* — the other half of the starter phrase
- *"All in one for everyone"* — William personally liked it ("there's something catchy about that")
- ✅ **"End to end control over your software factory"** — William's draft; ultimately retained
- *"Secure the software factory and all the stuff that you're making in it"* — Cindy's original elegant phrasing (from her 14.0 blog post)
- *"Secure the factory and its deliverables"* — William's condensed version of Cindy's line
- *"Secure and control your software factory and its deliverables"* — Brian's proposal to merge the security thought into the control line
  - Alternatives floated to replace "deliverables": *"your software," "your product," "your IP"* — none landed ("IP here… just doesn't sound right")

### Pillar 3 — Speed + Security / Velocity with Confidence
- *"Scale up, speed up, test up"* — starter phrase; immediately rejected
- *"Velocity with confidence"* — William's "channeling my inner Ash Withers" attempt
- *"Move fast with confidence"* — liked better than "Velocity with confidence"
- *"Increase speed and stay on track"* — from William's "bike race / race car" analogy
- *"More speed, less risk, high confidence"* — suggestion to append "high confidence"
- *"More speed, less risk, with confidence"* — alternative append
- *"No trade-offs"* — William's personal go-to; hotly debated
- *"More speed, less risk, no trade-offs"* — combo
- ✅ **"More speed, less risk"** — the final pick; described as "bam — no trade-offs, that's just how it is"

## 3. Evaluation Criteria the Team Used

The discussion surfaced several explicit criteria for judging phrases:

1. **Parity of tone / grammatical parallelism across the three pillars.**
   - Phrases needed to match in structure. William flagged this as a "super nuanced element of messaging": if one is a **noun phrase** (e.g., *"A single source of truth…," "End-to-end control…"* — "with GitLab you get X"), the others can't be **verb phrases** (e.g., *"Move fast with confidence"* — "with GitLab you can Y") without sounding lopsided.
2. **Pithiness / catchiness / punchiness.**
   - Goal: "a pithy few words, punchy statement" for each pillar. Short and memorable beat long explanatory phrases.
3. **Credibility — no absolute/untenable claims.**
   - Engineers recoil at statements like *"100% secure"* or *"no trade-offs"* because "there's never no trade-offs." Team preferred relative language like *"less risk"* / *"mitigate"* over absolutes.
4. **Avoiding overused, category-generic language.**
   - *"With confidence"* and *"high confidence"* were flagged as overused by competitors/vendors — generic B2B filler.
5. **Natural / idiomatic English.**
   - Rejected coinages that don't exist in real usage (e.g., *"test up"*: "I don't think anybody tests up. That's really goofy").
6. **Not sounding awkward or evoking the wrong metaphor.**
   - *"Deliverables"* made the software-factory metaphor tilt too far into car-manufacturing imagery; alternatives ("IP," "your product") also felt off.
7. **Bundling related value propositions cleanly.**
   - A good line should implicitly roll up adjacent benefits (e.g., *"less risk"* was seen to bundle *both* security *and* quality, avoiding the need for a separate "quality" mention).
8. **Individual taste vs. universal appeal.**
   - Explicitly acknowledged that "catchy is not everyone's cup of tea" — some phrases (e.g., *"All in one for everyone"*) were personally loved by some but not universally embraced.

## 4. Pros and Cons Discussed for Each Option

### Pillar 1 options
| Phrase | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| "From road map to company vision, we are transparent" | Provided as starting fodder | Not pithy or punchy; a sentence, not a tagline |
| "Collaborate on everything" | Snappy | Only half a thought; separated from its counterpart it loses meaning |
| **"A single source of truth, countless possibilities"** | "Really pithy and catchy"; widely liked; noun phrase that sets good parity for the rest | None strongly raised — became the default winner |

### Pillar 2 options
| Phrase | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| "Automate nearly anything" | Snappy verb phrase | Mismatched with pillar 1's noun phrase; spans into CI/CD territory rather than platform wholeness |
| "All in one for everyone" | "Something catchy about that" | "Catchy is not everyone's cup of tea"; too casual for some |
| **"End to end control over your software factory"** | Matches pillar 1's noun-phrase parity; evokes a clear platform metaphor | "Not as pithy" / "not as catchy" as the SSOT line; initially missing the security angle |
| "Secure the software factory and all the stuff that you're making in it" (Cindy original) | "Said really elegantly"; emphasizes that securing the *pipeline* and securing the *output* are two different things | Informal ("all the stuff you're making in it"); not a tight tagline |
| "Secure the factory and its deliverables" | Condenses Cindy's thought elegantly | "Deliverables" evokes car manufacturing; team didn't love the noun |
| "Secure and control your software factory and its deliverables" | Encompasses both the security and the control thought in one line; addresses the gap that "end-to-end control" understates security | Long and clunky; "deliverables" still awkward; substituting "your software / your product / IP" didn't sound right either |

### Pillar 3 options
| Phrase | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| "Scale up, speed up, test up" | Matches a parallel three-part structure | "I hate [it]… I don't think anybody tests up. That's really goofy." |
| "Velocity with confidence" | Attempt at catchy phrasing (Ash Withers style) | Felt forced; lost the race to a cleaner option |
| "Move fast with confidence" | Liked better than "velocity with confidence"; echoes Facebook's "move fast" but pairs it with safety | **Verb phrase** — breaks parity with the two noun-phrase pillars; "with confidence" is overused by competitors |
| "Increase speed and stay on track" | Came from a vivid race-car/bike analogy (testers/security "hold out a sign" that trips you up) | Nobody defended the phrasing itself; "I don't think I like that one" |
| "More speed, less risk, high confidence" / "…with confidence" | Attempts to add an extra beat | "High confidence / with confidence" is overused vendor-speak; unnecessary add-on |
| "No trade-offs" | "That's one of my go-to's, I love that one"; punchy | "The engineer in me says there's never no trade-offs — that's like saying 100% secure"; absolutist; not credible |
| **"More speed, less risk"** | Noun-like structure restores parity with pillars 1 & 2; "bam" punchy; "less risk" implicitly bundles security *and* quality ("I've talked myself out of not liking that"); avoids absolute claims; no overused words | None strongly raised in the room |

---

## 5. Final Selections

William closed the exercise with explicit decisions, noting the framework was **due at the end of the day**:

- **Pillar 1 (Visibility / SSOT):** *"A single source of truth, countless possibilities"*
- **Pillar 2 (Platform / Control):** *"End to end control over your software factory"* (kept as-is; Brian's "secure and control…" thought was appreciated but the wording never gelled, and the original was preferred)
- **Pillar 3 (Speed + Security):** *"More speed, less risk"* — William: *"I'm gonna go with 'More speed, less risk.'"*

The final set therefore achieved the grammatical parity William wanted — three "you get X" noun-style phrases:

> **A single source of truth, countless possibilities.**
> **End to end control over your software factory.**
> **More speed, less risk.**

William tagged the decisions as "strong" and moved on: *"Cool, those are good… this was kind of fun to do, a little bit of a working-together session."*

## 6. Rejected Alternatives and Why

| Rejected Phrase | Reason for Rejection |
|---|---|
| "Scale up, speed up, test up" | "Test up" is not idiomatic English — "goofy"; near-unanimous dislike ("I hate it") |
| "Velocity with confidence" | Less natural than "move fast with confidence"; superseded |
| "Move fast with confidence" | Breaks tone/grammar parity with the other two (verb phrase vs. noun phrase); "with confidence" is overused by competitors |
| "Increase speed and stay on track" | Clunky; the bike-race analogy didn't translate into a clean line; William: "I don't think I like that one" |
| "More speed, less risk, high confidence" / "…with confidence" | "With confidence" / "high confidence" is generic, overused vendor language; "more speed, less risk" stands on its own |
| "No trade-offs" | Logically/engineer-credible problem: there are always trade-offs; equivalent to claiming "100% secure"; team preferred the honest "less risk" framing |
| "All in one for everyone" | Too catchy / too casual for a universal framework line — "catchy isn't everyone's cup of tea" |
| "Automate nearly anything, collaborate on everything" | Splits across pillars rather than mapping one-to-a-box; verb phrases don't match the noun-phrase parity target |
| "From road map to company vision, we are transparent" | Too long; a descriptive sentence rather than a punchy tagline |
| "Secure the factory and its deliverables" / "Secure and control your software factory and its deliverables" | Strong concept (emphasize both the pipeline and what comes out of it), but "deliverables" evokes the wrong (car-manufacturing) metaphor; substitutes ("your software," "your product," "IP") all sounded off; couldn't land the wording in the time available |
