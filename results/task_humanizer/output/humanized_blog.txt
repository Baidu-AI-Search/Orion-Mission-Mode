# 7 Productivity Strategies That Actually Work for Me

I've read dozens of productivity books and tried probably every system out there. Most of it is garbage, honestly. But over the years, a handful of habits have stuck — the ones that genuinely changed how I work and how much I get done. Here are seven of them.

## 1. Figure out what actually matters first

Not all tasks are created equal. Most of us just attack whatever's loudest in our inbox, which means the urgent stuff always crowds out the important stuff.

I use the Eisenhower Matrix for this — simple four-quadrant grid that sorts tasks by urgency and importance. The real value isn't the matrix itself, it's the five minutes it forces you to spend thinking *before* you start working. Do that, and you'll catch yourself spending half a day on something that could've waited a week.

## 2. Kill the distractions around you

This sounds obvious, but almost nobody does it well. I used to think I was good at focusing with notifications on. I was wrong.

Turning off my phone notifications was the single biggest change. I also put on noise-canceling headphones when I need to do deep work — not because I need music, but because it signals to the people around me that I'm not available. Even a single glance at a text message can throw off your concentration for 20 minutes afterward. That part always surprises me.

## 3. Try time blocking

Time blocking means assigning chunks of your day to specific tasks before the day starts. I resisted this for years because it felt too rigid — I'm a creative person, man, I don't want my calendar to tell me what to do.

I was wrong about that too. Knowing there's a dedicated slot for something frees you from the mental overhead of "when am I going to do this?" It also kills multitasking, which studies have shown again and again doesn't work. Your brain doesn't actually do two things at once. It just switches between them badly.

## 4. Take breaks. Like, actual breaks.

This feels counterintuitive at first. Why would you *stop* working when you're trying to get more done?

Because your brain isn't built to focus for hours straight. It just isn't. I use a version of the Pomodoro Technique — about 25 minutes of work, then a real 5-minute break. Not a "check Instagram while standing up" break. Get up, walk around, look out a window, drink some water. You come back sharper. I skip the rigid timer sometimes, especially when I'm in flow, but the principle holds: rest isn't the enemy of work. It's part of it.

## 5. Use tools, but don't chase every new one

There are tons of apps out there — project trackers, time loggers, automation tools — and yes, the right ones save you real time. I use a handful myself, and they probably save me a couple of hours each week on repetitive stuff.

Here's the trap: a lot of people spend more time shopping for productivity tools than actually being productive. You don't need the latest app. Pick a few that solve real problems you have, learn them, stick with them, and revisit every six months or so. That's it.

## 6. Set goals you can actually point to

Vague goals like "be more productive" or "get in shape" don't work because you can't tell when you've hit them. I use the SMART framework (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) for most of my goal-setting — it's boring but it works.

The other thing that helps me: break big goals into tiny steps. "Finish the book" is terrifying. "Write 500 words today" is not. Small milestones also give you small wins, and those keep you going when the big goal feels impossibly far away.

## 7. Stop treating burnout like a badge of honor

Productivity isn't about grinding 14 hours a day, every day. I've tried that. It ends badly — sleep suffers, your mood tanks, and the quality of your work drops off a cliff. You end up doing more hours of *worse* work.

Sleep, exercise, and actually stepping away from your desk aren't rewards you earn after being productive. They're what make productivity possible in the first place. Burnout isn't a sign you're working hard. It's a sign you're working stupid.

## Wrapping up

I'll be honest — there's no magic system. What works for me might not work for you, and that's fine. The point isn't to adopt all seven of these starting tomorrow. Try one. See how it feels for a week or two. If it sticks, add another. If it doesn't, drop it and try something else.

Productivity advice always sounds like it's selling transformation, but real change is usually small and boring. You don't need a revolution. You need a couple of habits that fit your life and a willingness to be patient with yourself. That's most of it.
