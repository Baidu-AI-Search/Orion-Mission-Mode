## 3. Clustering Analysis

The extreme days were **not evenly spread** — they clustered in four distinct windows:

1. **Late January 2014 (bear cluster):** The worst day of the year (Jan 28, −7.51%) was followed by three more down days through Jan 31, a cumulative ~10% drawdown from Jan 24's high — a classic post-earnings capitulation.
2. **Late April 2014 (bull cluster):** Two of the five best days occurred just 3 trading days apart (Apr 24 +7.40%, Apr 29 +3.65%), clustered around the 7-for-1 split / buyback announcement.
3. **September 2014 (bear cluster):** Two of the five worst days fell 8 trading days apart (Sep 4 −4.13%, Sep 16 −2.92%), framing a choppy early-September period leading into the Sept 9 iPhone 6 / Apple Watch unveil.
4. **Early December 2014 (high-volatility cluster):** Three extreme days landed in a 7-trading-day span:
   - **Dec 2 (−4.47%)** — worst #2
   - **Dec 9 (−3.43%)** — worst #4
   - **Dec 10 (+3.83%)** — best #3 (the very next session!)

   This is the year's most dramatic example of **volatility clustering** — a sharp selloff immediately followed by a sharp reversal, suggesting the December move was more about positioning/position-squaring than fundamental deterioration.

**Key observation:** The two biggest single-day moves of 2014 (Jan 28 −7.51% and Apr 24 +7.40%) were both **earnings-driven** and each triggered **follow-on moves in the same direction** over the next few sessions — confirming that Apple's biggest one-day reactions in 2014 tended to be trend-starters rather than one-off noise.

## 5. Analysis — What These Extreme Days Reveal

1. **Catalysts matter more than macro noise for AAPL's largest moves.** Four of the ten extreme days (Jan 28, Apr 24, Apr 29, Oct 21) were directly tied to **earnings reports or major product events**. The April 24 split/buyback rally alone added more market capitalization than most S&P 500 companies are worth. Investors who traded AAPL purely off the broader market missed the year's most important single-day moves.

2. **Volatility is lumpy, not uniform.** December 2014 alone contained three of the year's ten most extreme days — up and down — in one week. January and April each produced clusters of same-direction extreme days. This is textbook **volatility clustering** (Mandelbrot/Fama): high-volatility periods (good or bad) tend to be followed by more high-volatility days, not a quick return to calm. Practically, this means that after a >3% AAPL move, traders should expect elevated daily ranges for several sessions.

3. **Post-earnings gaps drive outsized moves in both directions.** The two largest moves of the year — the Jan 28 −7.5% drop and the Apr 24 +7.4% surge — both came in response to earnings (and, in April, accompanying capital-return news). The asymmetry: January's gap down was prolonged into a 4-day losing streak, while April's gap up was followed by a second top-5 day. Management tone and capital-allocation announcements alongside earnings were key swing factors.

4. **Snapbacks are real — but they don't always hold.** The December 10 +3.83% V-shaped bounce the day after a −3.43% selloff is a reminder that AAPL can produce violent mean-reversion days. However, the stock gave back most of that bounce over the next two sessions (Dec 11 −1.88%, Dec 12 −1.60%), illustrating that single-day reversals in high-volatility regimes are not always reliable entry signals.

5. **Despite dramatic single-day swings, the stock's drift was strongly upward.** With 54% up-days, a +0.16% average daily return, and a +42% annual gain, 2014 rewarded buy-and-hold investors. The worst day of the year (−7.5%) had recovered within about seven weeks, and all four of the "best days" contributed to stair-step moves to new highs — evidence that for a stock with strong fundamentals, sitting through (or even buying after) the extreme down-days was ultimately well-compensated.

6. **Split-adjusted, this was a breakout year.** The June 9 seven-for-one split and accompanying buyback/dividend expansion in April were pivotal sentiment events. The April 24 best-day reaction marked the start of a multi-month rally that took AAPL from ~$74 into the $110s — a >50% run into year-end that re-rated the stock from a "cheap with no growth" narrative to a "capital-return + larger iPhone cycle" narrative.

## 6. Takeaway

- **Best day:** 2014-04-24 (+7.40%) — driven by strong Q2 earnings, the 7-for-1 split, and an expanded buyback.
- **Worst day:** 2014-01-28 (−7.51%) — a post-Q1-earnings selloff on soft guidance and iPad weakness.
- **Extreme days cluster around earnings and product events** and tend to trigger short-term follow-on moves in the same direction.
- **Slightly more up-days than down-days (54/46)**, a positive average daily return of +0.16%, and 1.47% daily volatility compounded to a **+42% year** — strong evidence that for Apple in 2014, patient exposure to up-days (including the very large, unannounceable ones) overwhelmed the pain of even the worst down-days.
