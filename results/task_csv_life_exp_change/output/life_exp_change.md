# Life Expectancy Change Over Time (1952–2007)

*Source: Gapminder dataset (n = 142 countries, 5 continents, observations every 5 years from 1952 to 2007).*

---

## 1. Global Trend

Worldwide average life expectancy (simple cross-country mean) rose steadily across the 55-year window, although the pace slowed noticeably after the mid-1980s.

| Year | Global avg. life expectancy (years) | 5-year change |
|------|-------------------------------------|---------------|
| 1952 | 49.06 | — |
| 1957 | 51.51 | +2.45 |
| 1962 | 53.61 | +2.10 |
| 1967 | 55.68 | +2.07 |
| 1972 | 57.65 | +1.97 |
| 1977 | 59.57 | +1.92 |
| 1982 | 61.53 | +1.96 |
| 1987 | 63.21 | +1.68 |
| 1992 | 64.16 | +0.95 |
| 1997 | 65.01 | +0.86 |
| 2002 | 65.70 | +0.68 |
| 2007 | 67.01 | +1.31 |

**Overall trajectory:** from **49.1 years in 1952 to 67.0 years in 2007**, a gain of **+17.9 years** — an average improvement of roughly 0.33 years per lived year. Gains were fastest in the 1950s–1970s (post-war public health, antibiotics, vaccines, the green revolution) and decelerated sharply after ~1987 as the HIV/AIDS crisis and conflict zones offset progress elsewhere. The population-weighted global average follows the same upward arc, ending at **68.9 years** in 2007 (slightly higher because populous Asian countries — especially China and India — improved faster than average).

| 1987 | 53.34 | 68.09 | 64.85 | 73.64 | 75.32 |
| 1992 | 53.63 | 69.57 | 66.54 | 74.44 | 76.95 |
| 1997 | 53.60 | 71.15 | 68.02 | 75.51 | 78.19 |

| 2007 | **54.81** | **73.61** | **70.73** | **77.65** | **80.72** |

**Total gain, 1952 → 2007 (years):**

| Continent | 1952 | 2007 | Δ |
|-----------|------|------|----|
| Asia      | 46.31 | 70.73 | **+24.41** |
| Africa    | 39.14 | 54.81 | +15.67 |
| Americas  | 53.28 | 73.61 | +20.33 |
| Europe    | 64.41 | 77.65 | +13.24 |
| Oceania   | 69.26 | 80.72 | +11.47 |

Key observations:

- **Asia** is the standout performer. It started well below the Americas and Europe, added more than 24 years, and by 2007 (70.7 years) was closing in on the Americas (73.6). The rapid gains reflect East Asia's development miracle plus dramatic improvements in South Asia and the Middle East.
- **Africa** showed steady improvement through 1987 (reaching 53.3 years) but then **stagnated for ~20 years** — life expectancy in 1992, 1997, and 2002 was *below* the 1987 level, almost entirely due to the HIV/AIDS epidemic and a wave of civil conflicts. A recovery is visible only by 2007 (54.8 years).
- **The Americas** progressed smoothly throughout, passing 70 years in the late 1990s.
- **Europe** and **Oceania** started from a high base and added ~11–13 years at a more gradual pace (diminishing returns at the frontier). Oceania led throughout, ending at 80.7 years.
- The ordering of continents has been remarkably stable: Oceania > Europe > Americas > Asia > Africa in every year, though Asia overtook what had been a large gap with the Americas.

## 5. Convergence or Divergence?

### Gap between the highest and lowest continent averages

| Year | Highest continent | Lowest continent | Gap (years) |
|------|-------------------|------------------|-------------|
| 1952 | Oceania (69.26) | Africa (39.14) | **30.12** |
| 1957 | Oceania (70.30) | Africa (41.27) | 29.03 |
| 1962 | Oceania (71.09) | Africa (43.32) | 27.77 |
| 1967 | Oceania (71.31) | Africa (45.34) | 25.98 |
| 1972 | Oceania (71.91) | Africa (47.45) | 24.46 |
| 1977 | Oceania (72.86) | Africa (49.58) | 23.28 |
| 1982 | Oceania (74.29) | Africa (51.59) | 22.70 |
| 1987 | Oceania (75.32) | Africa (53.34) | **21.98** (minimum) |
| 1992 | Oceania (76.95) | Africa (53.63) | 23.32 |
| 1997 | Oceania (78.19) | Africa (53.60) | 24.59 |
| 2002 | Oceania (79.74) | Africa (53.33) | 26.42 |
| 2007 | Oceania (80.72) | Africa (54.81) | 25.91 |

The story is a **U-shape**:

- **1952–1987: strong convergence.** The continent-level gap shrank by ~8 years (from 30.1 to 22.0), driven by rapid gains in Asia and Africa as basic public health (vaccines, antibiotics, clean water, malaria control) diffused globally, while the already-rich continents added years more slowly.
- **1987–2002: divergence resumed.** The gap *re-widened* by ~4.4 years, almost entirely because African life expectancy stalled (HIV/AIDS, conflict, economic stagnation) while every other continent kept climbing. The cross-country standard deviation tells the same story: it fell from 12.2 years in 1952 to a minimum of 10.6 in 1987, then rose back to 12.1 by 2007 — essentially undoing 30 years of cross-country convergence.
- **2002–2007: early signs of re-convergence.** Africa began a modest recovery as antiretroviral therapy scaled up, nudging the gap back down slightly.

**Bottom line:** the world saw impressive health convergence between mid-century and the late 1980s, but the HIV/AIDS crisis (and accompanying governance/economic failures in parts of Africa) reversed roughly half of that continent-level convergence by 2007. Whether at the continent level (Oceania vs. Africa) or country level, global health inequality remains large and is, if anything, slightly higher today than it was 50 years ago — despite the dramatic rise in the global average.

---

## 6. Summary of Key Takeaways

1. **Humanity as a whole gained ~18 years of life expectancy** between 1952 and 2007 (49 → 67 years), one of the most extraordinary achievements in human history. Gains were broad-based through the 1970s–80s but slowed after 1987.
2. **Asia was the star performer**, adding more than 24 years on average and producing 7 of the 10 fastest-improving countries (Oman, Vietnam, Indonesia, Saudi Arabia, South Korea, etc.).
3. **Africa is the tragedy.** It started last, closed some ground through the 1980s, then lost a decade (and more in Southern Africa) to HIV/AIDS, conflict, and state collapse. Nine of the ten worst-performing countries — including the only two that actually *lost* life expectancy (Zimbabwe, Swaziland) — are African.
4. **The world converged until ~1987 and then diverged again.** The continent-level gap narrowed from 30 years to 22 years by 1987, but widened back to ~26 years by 2007, leaving global cross-country inequality in life expectancy close to where it was in the 1950s despite the universal rise in averages.
5. **Leaders and laggards were remarkably persistent:** Oceania led every year; Africa trailed every year; and a small set of high-income countries (Norway, Iceland, Sweden, etc.) remained at the frontier throughout, with their slower gains reflecting natural ceiling effects rather than poor performance.
6. The overarching narrative is therefore a story of **spectacular progress combined with persistent — and, in recent decades, widening — inequality**: most of the world has experienced historically unprecedented improvements in longevity, but a concentrated set of (mostly Southern African) countries has been left behind or even gone backwards.
