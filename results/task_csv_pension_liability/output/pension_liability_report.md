## 5. Analysis: Where the Largest Future Financial Exposure Lies

### 5.1 Concentration of liability

Federal pension exposure is geographically concentrated. The 10 states in Section 4 account for roughly **61%** of all projected future state-level annual pension increases. A small number of states — California, New York, Ohio, Michigan, and Pennsylvania (the top five, totaling about **$1.18B** in additional annual payouts) — will drive the majority of growth in federal pension spending over the coming decades.

### 5.2 Two distinct drivers: headcount vs. average benefit

States reach the top of the projected-liability ranking through two mechanisms, sometimes combined:

- **Volume effect (large deferred populations).** New York, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Illinois, Texas, and New Jersey rank high primarily because they have very large absolute numbers of deferred federal workers. Their average payouts are close to or modestly above the national average (~$6,500), but sheer headcount translates into large future dollar outlays.

- **Benefit-composition effect (high average payouts).** States like Colorado ($9,711), Hawaii ($9,643), Washington ($9,224), Indiana ($8,434), Kansas ($8,196), Michigan ($8,058), and California ($8,023) are elevated because their retirees draw well above the national average, reflecting career-military (officer-heavy) and senior civil-service populations (defense commands, federal labs, VA networks, intelligence agencies). Every additional deferred worker in these states represents a larger future claim than the national norm.

- **Combined effect.** **California** and **Michigan** are notable for combining both drivers: large deferred populations *and* above-average payouts, pushing them to #1 and #4 on projected liability despite neither having the single largest deferred count nor the single highest average.

### 5.4 Structural explanation

Several structural factors explain the geographic pattern:

1. **Military footprint.** States with major Army, Navy, Air Force, Space Force, Marine Corps, or DoD-civilian installations — California, Texas, Virginia, Hawaii, Washington, Colorado, Florida, Georgia, Kansas — accumulate large deferred populations because active-duty members and DoD civilians earn future pension credits while stationed there. Military retirements (especially 20+ year careers and officers) push average payouts higher.

2. **Federal civilian employment hubs.** States with major federal regional offices, VA medical systems, postal networks, IRS campuses, and federal prisons — California, New York, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Illinois, Michigan, Texas, Florida, Indiana, New Jersey — generate high volumes of future claimants.

3. **Defense and national-security centers.** Colorado (NORAD, USSPACECOM, NORTHCOM, Air Force Academy, Denver Federal Center), Hawaii (INDOPACOM, Pearl Harbor, Schofield Barracks), Washington (Joint Base Lewis-McChord, Puget Sound Naval Shipyard, DOE Hanford), and Maryland (NSA/Fort Meade, NIH, FDA, Walter Reed, Aberdeen Proving Ground) combine senior-heavy workforces with long tenure, producing some of the highest average benefits in the nation.

4. **Population scale.** The country's largest states (CA, TX, FL, NY, PA, OH, IL) all appear near the top of the deferred-count rankings simply because of the scale of their employment base.

### 5.5 Bottom line for budget planning

- **Largest absolute future exposure:** **California, New York, Ohio, Michigan, and Pennsylvania** are the top five states by projected incremental annual pension liability, together representing approximately **$1.18B** in additional annual payouts when current deferred workers retire.

- **Highest average benefits:** **Colorado, Hawaii, Washington, Nevada, Wyoming, Montana, and Indiana** show average payouts 30–50% above the national average, so each deferred worker in those states represents a larger future claim than the national norm.

- **Fastest-growing future rolls:** **Washington, D.C.** is an extreme outlier (~569% deferred-to-current ratio). Among sizable states, **New Jersey (106.6%)**, **Utah (88.1%)**, **California (79.8%)**, **New York (78.5%)**, and **Colorado (76.1%)** are growing fastest.

- **Magnitude at the national level:** Approximately **$3.15B** in additional annual pension outlays is baked into the current deferred pipeline — roughly a 55% increase over today's $5.71B. The high-exposure states identified above should be treated as leading indicators of where federal pension cost growth will concentrate.

### Methodology & limitations

- **State rows:** State-level aggregate rows (where `STATE_ABBREV_NAME` ends in "Total"); the first row ("Grand Total") is used for national figures. Armed Forces – Americas / Europe / Pacific rows are included as reported.

- **Average payout per payee** = `PAYEE_AMOUNT` ÷ `PAYEE_COUNT` at the state aggregate row.

- **Projected future annual liability (state)** = state average payout × `DEFERRED_COUNT` for that state.

- **National projected liability** = national average payout × national deferred count, computed from the Grand Total row.

- **Caveats:** This is a static, first-order estimate. It does not model cost-of-living adjustments (COLA), future salary growth, mortality, discounting, take-up rates, changes in retirement eligibility, or potential benefit reforms. Actual future outlays will differ, but the relative ranking of state-level exposure is directionally informative.
