# 2014 World GDP Regional Analysis

This report categorizes 222 countries and territories from the 2014 GDP dataset into eight geographic regions and analyzes the distribution of global economic output. All GDP figures are in billions of US dollars (nominal).

## Notes on Regional Classification

Several countries span multiple continents or have ambiguous regional affiliations. The following borderline conventions were applied:

- **Russia** → Europe: Roughly 75% of Russia's population and the bulk of its economic activity lie in European Russia; it is integrated into European economic/political institutions (Council of Europe, OSCE).

- **Turkey** → Europe: Turkey is an EU accession candidate, a NATO member, and maintains deep institutional and economic ties with Europe; an alternative placement could be Middle East & North Africa.

- **Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan (Caucasus)** → Central Asia & Caucasus: These transcontinental states share post-Soviet heritage and strong political/economic linkages with Central Asia.

- **Cyprus** → Europe: EU member since 2004.

- **Israel** → Middle East & North Africa: Geographically in the Levant.

- **Afghanistan** → South Asia: SAARC member.

- **Greenland** → North America: Geographically part of North America (though politically tied to Denmark/Europe).

- Small overseas territories (Bermuda, Cayman Islands, Falklands, French Polynesia, etc.) are assigned to the region they geographically belong to.

## 3. Regional Comparison: Dominance of the Global Economy

The three regions that dominate the global economy are **Europe** (29.16%), **East Asia & Pacific** (27.66%), and **North America** (24.55%). Together they account for **81.36%** of world GDP.

This concentration reflects the fact that the world's largest economies — the United States, China, Japan, Germany, the United Kingdom, and France — are clustered in these three regions, which together host the principal engines of global finance, manufacturing, and technology.

- **Highest average**: **North America** at an average of $6,405.39 B per country. This is driven by the presence of very large advanced economies (e.g., the United States at $17,420 B and Canada at $1,794 B) with only a handful of small territories, pulling the average sharply upward.

- **Lowest average**: **Sub-Saharan Africa** at an average of $35.08 B per country. This region contains many small, developing, and often landlocked or island economies whose output is several orders of magnitude below that of the world's largest economies.

This indicates a **strong skew in global output**: a small number of large economies in a few regions dramatically outpace the rest, while regions with many small developing states exhibit much lower average economic mass per country.

## 6. Summary: Geographic Distribution of Global Economic Output

Global economic output in 2014 was heavily concentrated in three macro-regions — **Europe**, **East Asia & Pacific**, and **North America** — which together produced **81.36%** of world GDP. 
North America alone, despite comprising only three economies, contributed a disproportionately large share due to the outsized weight of the United States. 
East Asia & Pacific was the second-largest region, anchored by China, Japan, and South Korea but spanning a staggering range from major industrial powers to Pacific island microstates. 
Europe, with its dense cluster of advanced, high-income economies (Germany, UK, France, Italy, etc.), represented the third pillar. 
Meanwhile, Latin America & Caribbean, Middle East & North Africa, and Sub-Saharan Africa each contributed modest single-digit-to-low-double-digit shares, with Sub-Saharan Africa hosting the largest number of countries but producing one of the smallest aggregate outputs — a reflection of both its many small economies and generally lower per-capita productivity in 2014. 
South Asia was dominated by India, and Central Asia & Caucasus, though resource-rich, remained the smallest regional economic bloc. Overall, the picture is one of pronounced geographic concentration: a handful of regions — and within them, a handful of countries — generate the vast majority of global GDP, while numerous small economies in Africa, the Caribbean, and the Pacific contribute only marginally to global output.
