# GDP Per Capita Estimates — World's Top 30 Economies (2014)

## Methodology Note

The source file `world_gdp_2014.csv` contains total GDP (in billions of current US dollars) for 222 countries and territories, along with country names and ISO codes, but **does not include population data**. To estimate GDP per capita for the 30 largest economies by total GDP, approximate **mid-year 2014 populations** were drawn from widely used public sources (UN World Population Prospects, World Bank, U.S. Census Bureau IDB, and CIA World Factbook mid-2014 estimates). Because population figures are rounded to one decimal place in millions, the resulting per-capita values should be treated as **order-of-magnitude estimates** accurate to within roughly ±5%—sufficient for cross-country comparisons and ranking, but not for precise statistical inference.

GDP per capita is calculated as:

> **GDP per capita (USD) = (GDP in billions × 10⁹) / (population in millions × 10⁶)**

## 3. Key Observations

### Highest per-capita incomes among the top 30
The **top five** by estimated GDP per capita are **Norway (~$100k), Switzerland (~$84k), Australia (~$63k), Sweden (~$58k), and the United States (~$55k)**. With the exception of the U.S.—which combines massive scale with high productivity—all are small-to-mid-sized, high-productivity economies:
- **Norway** benefits from a tiny population (≈5.1 million) atop enormous North Sea oil and gas reserves channeled through its sovereign wealth fund.
- **Switzerland** is a global hub for finance, pharmaceuticals, and high-value manufacturing with just 8 million people.
- **Australia** and **Sweden** both combine resource endowments and/or advanced industrial bases with populations under 25 million.

### The U.S. and other large economies
- The **United States** is the only country in the top 5 of **both** rankings, reflecting the rare combination of economic size *and* high average living standards.
- **Germany, the UK, and France** all cluster in the $43k–$47k per-capita band (positions 9–13), solidly upper-income but clearly behind the smaller, resource- or finance-rich leaders.
- **Japan** ($37.5k) and **Italy** ($35k) retain high living standards but trail the Anglo-Saxon and Northern European peers—a reflection of two decades of slow nominal growth (Japan) and post-euro stagnation (Italy).

### Lowest per-capita incomes among the top 30
The **bottom four**—**India (~$1,600), Nigeria (~$3,300), Indonesia (~$3,400), and Iran (~$5,200)**—are populous emerging markets where aggregate economic weight comes from headcount rather than productivity.
- **India**, despite being the **#10 economy** overall, ranks dead last among the 30 in per-capita terms—its 1.27 billion people produce a total GDP comparable to Russia's, but spread across nearly 9× the population.
- **China** ($7.6k) sits at #26 per capita. It is the world's **#2 economy** but remains an upper-middle-income country, with living standards roughly one-seventh of the U.S. level and one-thirteenth of Norway's.
- **Brazil ($11k), Mexico ($10.8k), and Turkey ($10.6k)**—often cited as emerging-market success stories—still deliver per-capita incomes only about one-fifth of U.S. levels.

### What this reveals about economic size vs. living standards
**Total GDP is a measure of market size and geopolitical weight; GDP per capita is a (crude) proxy for average living standards and productivity.** The two tell very different stories:
- Countries with populations under ~20 million dominate the top of the per-capita table because concentrated wealth (resources, finance, high-value exports) is shared across fewer people.
- The demographic giants (China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, Nigeria, Russia) are globally influential because of their sheer scale, but their average citizens remain substantially poorer than those in Western Europe, North America, Japan, or the smaller high-income economies.
- The only economy that reconciles enormous population *and* high per-capita income is the **United States**—a fact that underpins its unique position in the global system.

| **UAE** | 29 | 11 | **+18** | Hydrocarbon wealth concentrated among ~9 million residents (of whom only ~1 million are citizens) produces very high *statistical* per-capita GDP, though it is unevenly distributed. |

### Honorable mentions
- **Australia (+9)** and **Canada (+3)** both combine large landmasses and resource wealth with relatively small populations, landing them well inside the top 10 per capita.
- **Saudi Arabia (Δ ≈ 0)** and **Taiwan (+6)** are roughly where one would expect given their size—middle- to upper-income for their GDP weight.
- **Mexico (−9)**, **Turkey (−7)**, and **Nigeria (−8)** are classic cases of large-population emerging markets where aggregate size hides modest per-person output.

---

## 5. Summary Take-away

Of the world's 30 largest economies in 2014:
- **Five** delivered per-capita GDP above $50,000: Norway, Switzerland, Australia, Sweden, and the United States.
- **Four** had per-capita GDP below $6,000: India, Nigeria, Indonesia, and Iran.
- The gap between the richest (Norway, ~$100k) and the poorest (India, ~$1.6k) among this group exceeds **60×**.
- Population size is the dominant variable explaining these gaps: small, productive, often resource- or finance-rich countries dominate the per-capita rankings, while demographic giants dominate the total-GDP rankings. The U.S. is the only country that sits at the very top of both lists.
